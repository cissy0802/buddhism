Sutra Studies: The Four Schools of Tibetan Buddhism

One mind, four gates — differing in view and method, one in goal

Nyingma · Dzogchen (Atiyoga)

The Three Statements That Strike the Vital Point

By Garab Dorje · c. 2nd century CE · Commentary by Patrul Rinpoche (19th c.)

Scripture

"Directly recognize your own true face; decide firmly upon this alone; gain confidence in liberation." First: through the master's direct pointing, recognize rigpa (thought-free awareness itself) here and now. Second: decide there is nothing else to seek but this awareness. Third: the moment a thought arises, it self-liberates within awareness—gain unshakable confidence in this. These three lines distill the entire Dzogchen path. (From Tsik Sum Nedek, tshig gsum gnad brdeg)

Exposition

Dzogchen (rDzogs chen), the Nyingma school's highest teaching, traces to Padmasambhava's arrival in Tibet (8th c.) and was systematized by Longchenpa (1308–1364). Its view: mind's nature is primordially perfect—both originally pure (kadag) and spontaneously present (lhundrup), requiring no fabrication.

Its methods are twofold: trekchö (cutting through) directly severs the root of thought and rests in primordial purity; tögal (leaping over) lets luminosity spontaneously display upon that ground. The key is distinguishing "mind" (sems, the stream of thought) from "awareness" (rigpa, thought-free knowing).

Garab Dorje was the first human Dzogchen master; the "Three Statements" were his parting testament to Mañjuśrīmitra—recognize, decide, liberate—one continuous thread.

Cross-disciplinary Resonance

With consciousness studies: Dzogchen's "awareness" is pure, objectless knowing—awareness itself, pointing to no content. This strongly resonates with contemporary research on "pure/contentless awareness": in deep meditation the default mode network (DMN) quiets while the sheer "presence of awareness" persists. Awareness vs. its contents maps precisely onto "knowing itself vs. what is known"—still the hard problem consciousness science cannot resolve.

Practice in Life

Traditional: After the master's pointing-out, the student repeatedly "recognizes" rigpa in daily life, gradually lengthening the gaps of recognition.

Modern application: When emotion strikes (a rejected proposal, a crying child), neither suppress nor follow it—for a single instant, look back at the very "awareness" that is anxious. Not analyzing the anxiety, but recognizing the knowing that knows it. The anxiety loosens by itself within awareness, like a wave returning to water.

Daily Practice

The "Look Back" exercise: Three times today, when a thought arises (a worry, an irritation, a craving), don't engage its content—just ask once, "What is it that knows this thought?" Rest on that "knower" for a second or two. Watch whether the thought releases on its own.
Kagyu · Mahamudra

The Ganges Mahamudra

Tilopa to Naropa · c. 10th–11th century · Chinese trans. by Garma C.C. Chang (20th c.)

Scripture

"Just as space depends on nothing, Mahamudra too rests on no support. Relax and settle into the natural state, uncontrived, uncorrected—and the bound one frees himself." Just as space leans on no single thing, Mahamudra (mind's true reality) likewise abides without support. Simply rest, relaxed, in the natural state—adding no correction, making no choice—and bondage dissolves of itself. (From the pith instructions Tilopa gave Naropa on the Ganges' bank.)

Exposition

Mahamudra (phyag rgya chen po) is the Kagyu heart practice. Tilopa transmitted it to Naropa on the Ganges, and it reached Tibet through Marpa—Milarepa—Gampopa. Gampopa (1079–1153) fused the Kadam graded path, the Six Yogas of Naropa, and Mahamudra into one system, founding the school's scale.

The view: mind's nature is innately luminous; thoughts, like waves inseparable from water, need not be cut off but self-liberate (rang grol). Practice lies not in fabrication but in one word—"relax": free of correction, free of accept-and-reject, simply recognize the natural state. The path unfolds in four yogas: one-pointedness, simplicity, one-taste, non-meditation.

Cross-disciplinary Resonance

With psychology: "Thoughts self-liberate" precisely matches cognitive defusion in Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT): don't identify with a thought, don't push or pull it, and it loses its adhesive grip and dissolves. "Relax and settle" is close to what mindfulness research calls decentering—stepping back from "I am this thought" to the metacognitive stance of "I am aware of this thought."

Practice in Life

Traditional: The practitioner follows the four yogas—first steadying the mind through one-pointedness, then advancing to simplicity and one-taste, and finally to effortless non-meditation.

Modern application: When a thought keeps snagging you (regret over something said, worry over a project), don't analyze or suppress—just say to it quietly, "I see you," and let it pass like subtitles scrolling across a screen: neither grasped nor pushed away, it arises and vanishes on its own.

Daily Practice

The "Subtitle" exercise: This week pick the thought that most loves to ensnare you. Each time it appears, picture it as a line of subtitles scrolling along the bottom of a screen: see it, read it, let it slide off—no chasing, no deleting. Note how many times a day it comes, and feel its grip weaken.
Sakya · Lamdre (Path and Result)

The Vajra Verses (root of Lamdre)

By Virupa · c. 9th century · Transmitted by Drokmi · Founded by Sachen Kunga Nyingpo

Scripture

"The impure appearance, the experiential appearance, and the pure appearance are all displays of one's own mind; samsara and nirvana are without duality, without distinction." The defiled world seen by ordinary beings (impure appearance), the experiential realm attained by yogis (experiential appearance), and the pure buddha-fields seen by buddhas (pure appearance) are three faces of one mind's display; samsara and nirvana are not two separate realities—their nature is without distinction. (The "three appearances" and "inseparability of samsara and nirvana," from Virupa's Vajra Verses, rdo rje'i tshig rkang.)

Exposition

Lamdre ("Path and Result") is the Sakya school's uncommon teaching, sourced from the mahasiddha Virupa, transmitted to Tibet by the translator Drokmi and grounded by Sachen Kunga Nyingpo's (1092–1158) commentaries. Its core view, the inseparability of samsara and nirvana ('khor 'das dbyer med): what is seen in delusion is impure samsara, in practice turns to experience, and in awakening displays as purity—one mind-nature, three appearances, no separate substance.

Its framework threads "the three continua"—cause, path, and fruit: the ground continuum is the ālaya as the basis of practice; the path continuum purifies obscurations; the fruit continuum reveals innate buddha-qualities. Lamdre is neither a pure sudden discarding of the conventional (unlike Dzogchen) nor purely gradual, but a tantric path where view and practice are one, taking the fruit's awareness as the ground of the path.

Cross-disciplinary Resonance

With phenomenology & predictive processing: The "three appearances" reveal that the same sensory input, framed by different generative models of the mind, manifests radically different worlds—pure or impure. This deeply echoes predictive-processing theory: perception is the brain's active construction of a model, not passive reception. A world's defilement lies not in the object but in the mind that manifests it. To turn the mind is to turn the world—not idle philosophy, but the operative mechanism of perception.

Practice in Life

Traditional: The Sakya practitioner follows the sequence of the three appearances, progressively purifying what is seen until the defiled scene turns to pure experience.

Modern application: Facing the same situation (a traffic jam, a rejected manuscript, endless chores), practice "switching the appearance"—first honestly notice that right now you're in the "impure appearance" (annoyance), without denying it; then ask, "Does this annoyance come from the object or from my mind?" The instant you see the scene unchanged yet the mind able to shift—that is the path in use.

Daily Practice

The "switch appearance" three questions: This week, when something clearly annoys you, ask in turn—(1) Which appearance am I in right now? (2) Is this defilement inherent in the object, or added by mind? (3) With a different pair of eyes, what use could this scene serve? Don't demand an instant shift—just practice seeing "the scene unmoved, yet the mind able to move."
Gelug · The Graded Path

The Three Principal Aspects of the Path (lam gtso rnam gsum)

By Tsongkhapa · 14th–15th century

Scripture

"Without renunciation there is no way to still the pursuit of pleasure within the ocean of existence; craving for existence binds embodied beings—so first seek renunciation."

"When these two arise together without alternation—seeing dependent arising as undeceiving while it demolishes every fixation on the real—then the analysis into emptiness is complete." Without renunciation, one cannot still the inverted drive to "seek happiness within the ocean of birth-and-death"; it is precisely this craving for the three realms' pleasures that binds beings in samsara, so renunciation must come first. When "dependent appearance" and "emptiness of self-nature" arise simultaneously, no longer alternating (not one-then-the-other), seeing that cause-and-effect is not the least bit false while simultaneously demolishing every apprehension of "real existence"—then and only then is the wisdom that analyzes emptiness complete.

Exposition

Tsongkhapa (1357–1419), founder of the Gelug school, emphasized discipline and gradation and firmly opposed skipping stages. The Three Principal Aspects condenses the whole path into a few verses—the marrow of his Great Treatise on the Stages of the Path: renunciation (weariness of samsara), bodhicitta (vowing buddhahood for beings' sake), and correct view (the Prasangika Madhyamaka view of non-self-nature).

None can be omitted: without renunciation, practice yields mere worldly merit; without bodhicitta, even realizing emptiness falls to the lesser vehicle; without correct view, the root of samsara is never cut. The crux of correct view is "dependent arising and emptiness in union"—precisely because things lack self-nature, causal dependent arising is vividly undeceiving; the two do not contradict but mutually establish each other. This is Gelug's most rigorous grounding of Madhyamaka (Day 2).

Cross-disciplinary Resonance

With AI value alignment: The Three Principal Aspects form a clear "motivation–view" hierarchy: renunciation = removing the wrong utility function (the meta-drive to escape suffering); bodhicitta = an altruistic goal-setting toward others; correct view = holding no reified grasp of the goal itself, avoiding "Goodhart-style" metric-rigidity. A truly "awakened agent" needs all three tiers—a correct meta-goal, an altruistic target, and freedom from being devoured by its own self-set metrics.

Practice in Life

Traditional: Gelug monks train through the "three scopes" of the Great Treatise: first the small scope (seeking a higher rebirth), then the middling (arousing renunciation), then the great (arousing bodhicitta).

Modern application: Each morning, orient the day with three questions—(1) Is the "ocean-pleasure" I'm chasing truly worth it? (renunciation) (2) Can today's effort also benefit one other person? (bodhicitta) (3) Does the "result" I cling to have any independent self-nature? (correct view). Then step out the door.

Daily Practice

The "three-question orientation" morning ritual: This week, before leaving home each day, silently ask three lines—Is what I seek worth it (renunciation)? Can I benefit others today (bodhicitta)? Does the result I grasp have self-nature (correct view)? Pause three breaths on each. After a week, look back: which question stung most? That is exactly your present work.

Deep Reflection

Dialogue across schools, differences of transmission, cross-disciplinary mapping, the boundaries of practice
1. Dzogchen and Mahamudra teach "sudden recognition of awareness," while Gelug insists on strict gradual training—is this a difference of capacity, or a dispute over the ultimate view?
Better seen as a difference of pedagogical sequence, not a dispute over ultimate view. Dzogchen/Mahamudra take the "fruition-vehicle" approach, using innate buddha-nature as the ground of the path, hence direct pointing and sudden entry—yet tradition equally requires preliminaries (ngöndro) to gather merit and purify, plus the master's pointing-out and empowerment; it is no baseless "suddenness." Gelug takes the steady "causal-vehicle" approach; Tsongkhapa, wary of degenerate-age practitioners skipping stages and deceiving themselves with mere semblances of realization, laid down the graded three-scope path. On "mind's nature is originally pure" the two do not disagree—what they debate is how to arrive safely. The Rimé (non-sectarian) movement holds that sudden and gradual are two skillful means fitted to capacity; a practitioner should choose by their own continuum, not use one to judge the other.
2. All four schools say "mind's nature is originally luminous and pure"—does this, versus Theravada's thoroughgoing "no-self" (Day 26) and Chinese tathāgatagarbha (Day 22), relapse into a "true self"?
The verbal tension is real, but the meaning reconciles without being flattened. The "luminous awareness" (rigpa / 'od gsal) of Tibetan Buddhism is not the substantial divine self of non-Buddhist schools: it is without self-nature, free of subject-object, not to be grasped as any "thing"—exactly what the Three Statements' "decide firmly" guards against, since the moment awareness is taken as a graspable "self," one has fallen into error. This is the same defense line as tathāgatagarbha's "self that is no-self" (the suchness beyond the tetralemma), and it converges with Theravada's "no-self" at the point of "freedom from all conceptual proliferation." The difference is an emphasis on affirmation vs. negation: Theravada stresses negation (exhausting all to reveal), Tibetan tantra stresses affirmation (positively indicating luminosity to begin practice). The emphasis differs; the landing point—freedom from grasping—is the same. Read "luminosity" as a substance, and both traditions judge it a disease.
3. Is Dzogchen's "awareness" (contentless knowing) the same referent as consciousness science's "pure awareness / minimal self"?
A moving resonance, but not to be hastily equated. Similarities: both point to an "objectless presence of knowing," and both report the dissolution of self-boundaries and the default network in deep meditation. Two differences: first, rigpa is a first-person direct realization, judged to be "just as it is, not produced by practice"; science's "pure awareness" is a measurable state, not necessarily identical to the unborn, unceasing essence Dzogchen indicates. Second, the aims differ—rigpa's landing point is liberation and the manifesting of innate qualities; science's is explaining neural mechanism. They can inspire each other, but directly "translating" realization into brain states, or using fMRI to "prove" rigpa, oversteps the line—precisely the trap Day 31 will examine.
4. Lamdre's "inseparability of samsara and nirvana" and Mahamudra's "self-liberating thoughts"—if affliction is itself awakening, need one still abandon evil and cultivate good? Could it become a license for indulgence?
This is where tantra is most easily misread, and it demands sharp distinction. "Samsara and nirvana are inseparable," "affliction is awakening," are fruition-level views, describing the reality a realized one directly beholds—not a behavioral permit for the ordinary being. At the causal stage, none of the four schools discards renunciation, discipline, or the accumulation of merit and purification—Gelug takes discipline as its very lifeblood, and the formal training of Nyingma, Kagyu, and Sakya likewise gates on preliminaries. Genuine "self-liberation" presupposes having recognized awareness / having generated concentration and wisdom; only then can a rising thought dissolve within awareness. Let thoughts run wild without recognizing awareness, and that is not liberation but distraction. Using "affliction is awakening" to gloss over faults and indulge desire is precisely the inversion that mistakes the definitive meaning for license—something the tantric samaya vows penalize most severely.
5. The union imagery and wrathful deities of highest tantra are often read literally; does the "secrecy" tradition still hold in the information age?
Worth facing honestly. Secrecy in tantra has two intents: one, to protect the student—those without the view and empowerment easily take union, wrath, and other symbols literally, turning them into causes of slander or lust; two, to protect the teaching—the pith instructions within the guru-disciple continuum must be sealed mind-to-mind and calibrated to the moment, and text stripped of context is easily distorted. In an age where texts are a click away, "secrecy" shifts from "the text being invisible" to "the context and lineage being irreplaceable": to have read is not to have received; empowerment, samaya, and verified transmission remain tantra's indispensable threshold. Compared with Gelug's openly published graded Treatise, this very tension is the division of labor—"the exoteric widely taught to lay the foundation, the esoteric orally conferred to verify the continuum"—not a matter of higher and lower.
