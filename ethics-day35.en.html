Mahāyāna Vinaya · Bodhisattva Precepts

The Brahmajāla Sūtra's Bodhisattva Precepts (Non-killing & Vegetarianism)

Attr. Kumārajīva · foundation of Chinese bodhisattva ordination · 5th c.

Scripture Passage

"A disciple of the Buddha must not intentionally kill any living being. One should give rise to an ever-abiding mind of compassion and filial care, and by every means protect and rescue all beings. To kill instead, indulging one's own delight—this is a pārājika (defeat) offense for a bodhisattva."

"One who eats meat severs the seed of great compassion, the seed of Buddha-nature; all beings, seeing him, flee in fear. Therefore no bodhisattva may eat the flesh of any being." — From the "Chapter on the Mind-Ground Precepts," lower fascicle.

Commentary

The Brahmajāla's lower fascicle is the backbone of the East Asian Mahāyāna precepts, establishing ten grave and forty-eight minor precepts. Non-killing stands first among the ten, and it extends the scope from "humans" to "all living beings"—Buddhist ethics' most fundamental coordinate: the boundary of moral concern is as wide as the capacity to suffer, not as narrow as "being human" or "being of my kind."

Note carefully: Chinese vegetarianism is not the Buddha's original rule but a Mahāyāna development. Theravāda and Tibetan traditions still permit "threefold-pure meat" (not seen, heard, or suspected to be killed for oneself). Vegetarianism became an East Asian absolute largely because Emperor Wu of Liang, citing this sūtra and the Laṅkāvatāra, abolished meat and wine by imperial decree. This reminds us that precepts are historically generated; grasping their "conditions" keeps us from dogmatism.

Cross-Disciplinary Resonance

Animal ethics: Peter Singer draws the boundary of moral standing at sentience—the capacity to suffer—and condemns "speciesism" as akin to racism. This is strikingly isomorphic with the sūtra's "all living beings," except Buddhism arrived fifteen centuries earlier and grounds it in compassion rather than utility.

Environmental science: Animal agriculture accounts for a substantial share of human greenhouse emissions and staggering water and land use. Today's climate case for plant-based eating gives the ancient meat-precept a wholly new, non-religious support.

Living Practice

Traditional: Monastic communities eat before noon and vegetarian; lay bodhisattva-precept holders abstain from meat for life or on fixed days.

Modern: No need to leap all at once. Begin with "Meatless Monday" or one plant-based meal a day, treating it as a practice of widening the radius of empathy—pausing a second at each choice to see the life and carbon footprint behind the food. Ethics is not an all-or-nothing purism but a steady direction of shrinking the suffering one causes.

Daily Cultivation

Contemplating one meal: Before one meal today, silently reflect on where the food came from—whose life it once was, how much water and soil it took, how many hands it passed through. Not for guilt, only to "see." The seeing itself softens the mind bent on "indulgent delight."

The Cakkavatti-Sīhanāda Sutta (War & Justice)

Tr. Buddhayaśas & Zhu Fonian · Pāli parallel DN 26 · early canon

Scripture Passage

"At that time many among the people were poor; because of poverty they took to theft and plunder; because plunder spread, weapons multiplied by the day; as weapons multiplied, killing grew ever more rampant… and so the lifespan of beings shrank and their features coarsened." — The root cause: former kings had "given to the destitute," but the later king abolished this policy and relied on punishment alone, driving the vicious cycle.

Commentary

Through the rise and fall of the "wheel-turning monarch" (the ideal ruler who unifies the world by Dharma, not force), the sutta teaches a profound genesis of violence: turmoil begins not with innate evil but with distributive failure. When rulers fail to "give wealth to those who lack," poverty breeds theft, theft invites harsh punishment, punishment provokes lies and greater violence, and society degrades layer by layer.

Buddhism is not empty unconditional pacifism: it digs the roots of war down to economy and governance, holding that true security comes from eliminating structural scarcity, not from meeting sword with sword. The wheel-turner conquers "without rod or sword, by Dharma"—Buddhism's positive vision of just rule: justice precedes order; compassion is realized as institution.

Cross-Disciplinary Resonance

Peace studies: Johan Galtung's "structural violence"—the harm embedded in institutions like poverty and inequality that maims without a single weapon—is almost a modern restatement of this sutta. Real (positive) peace is not the absence of war but the absence of structural deprivation.

Criminology: Extensive evidence links violent crime to economic deprivation and inequality. The sutta's causal chain—"poverty → theft → weapons"—points in the same direction as the core findings of modern criminal sociology.

Living Practice

Traditional: This sutta is a fountainhead of Buddhist political ethics, invoked across the ages to urge rulers toward benevolent governance and care for the poor.

Modern: Facing conflict (team friction, family quarrels, even public disputes), resist rushing to ask "who is the villain," and ask instead: what "scarcity" is manufacturing this opposition?—a shortage of resources, of recognition, or of a sense of safety? Supplying that lack, within your reach, often ends strife more truly than assigning blame.

Daily Cultivation

Tracing a conflict to its source: Recall a recent quarrel and write three layers: "What was ostensibly fought over?" "What was lacking underneath (money, respect, control, safety)?" "Which portion can I supply?" Practice shifting "stopping violence" from confrontation to "filling the gap."

Sūtra on the Eight Realizations of Great Beings (Contentment & Ecological Buddhism)

Tr. An Shigao (Later Han) · among the earliest Chinese sūtras · 2nd c.

Scripture Passage

"The second realization: much desire is suffering. The weariness of birth-and-death arises from craving; with few desires and non-contriving, body and mind are at ease."

"The third realization: a mind never satisfied, seeking only to gain more, multiplies wrongdoing. The bodhisattva is not so: ever mindful of contentment, at peace in simplicity, guarding the Way, making wisdom alone their work." — The Second and Third of the Eight Realizations.

Commentary

This sūtra gathers all practice under eight things "the great (awakened) realize"—minimal yet complete. Its second and third realizations point directly at "much desire" and "no satisfaction" as the roots of suffering and wrongdoing. Originally a psychology of liberation, it has unexpectedly become the firmest scriptural ground for contemporary ecological Buddhism.

The engine of today's ecological crisis is precisely "a mind never satisfied, seeking only to gain more"—an infinite growth-desire institutionalized and amplified by advertising. Rather than entering through the Western frame of "human-centered vs. nature-centered," Buddhism strikes at the root by questioning the infinity of desire itself: a sustainable outer world presupposes the inner turn of "few desires, contentment." "At peace in simplicity" is no cult of asceticism but a revolution of value—from "more" to "enough."

Cross-Disciplinary Resonance

Ecological economics: "Contentment" echoes the "steady-state economy" and "doughnut economics"—acknowledging the planet's ecological ceiling, prosperity is not infinite growth. Buddhism supplies the inner ground for "enough is good."

Behavioral economics: "A mind never satisfied" is the "hedonic treadmill"—after a desire is met, the baseline rises, joy soon returns to zero, and one must seek again. "Few desires" is the only exit off that treadmill; here experimental psychology and the sūtra meet.

Living Practice

Traditional: Short and easy to recite, this sūtra has long served as a daily self-admonishing lesson for monastics and laity alike.

Modern: Try a "defining enough" exercise: before buying, ask—"Is what I already have already enough?"—and halt one purchase you would otherwise have clicked. Ecological practice need not be grand; it is often just the small, clear act of "not buying," replacing the momentum of "more" with the freedom of "enough."

Daily Cultivation

An "enough" day: Today, acquire nothing non-essential, and when the impulse to buy stirs, notice where that "sense of lack" arises. In the evening, note: what did I "think I needed but actually did not" today? Taste "few desires, non-contriving—body and mind at ease."
Impermanence · Bedside Recitation

The Sūtra on Impermanence (a.k.a. "Three Exhortations" · End-of-Life Care)

Tr. Yijing (Tang) · traditional deathbed recitation text · 8th c.

Scripture Passage

"All that is born returns to death; the face must change and wither; sickness invades even the strong—none can escape this.
Even Mount Sumeru, at the aeon's end, crumbles and scatters; the ocean, deep and bottomless, likewise runs dry.
The great earth, the sun and moon—when the time comes, all reach their end. Never has there been one single thing not devoured by impermanence." — The opening verses of the sūtra.

Commentary

Structured in "three exhortations" (opening praise, main text, closing praise), the Sūtra on Impermanence has, since the Tang, been a common recitation text for the dying and for funeral rites—it does not evade death but places it plainly before the dying and their companions. This is the core posture of Buddhist end-of-life care: calm, direct facing rather than concealment and cosmetics.

Buddhism regards death as part of practice, not mere medical failure, and works to cultivate a "good death"—letting go of attachment, recalling the wholesome, keeping the mind unconfused. This resonates deeply with modern palliative care: when cure is no longer possible, medicine's aim turns to easing pain, preserving dignity, and settling body and mind. The sūtra's plainness is itself a compassion—it honors the dying with the respect of "seeing things as they are."

Cross-Disciplinary Resonance

Palliative care: The hospice movement pioneered by Cicely Saunders attends to "total pain"—physical, psychological, social, and spiritual—closely matching the Buddhist orientation of tending both bodily suffering and the settling of consciousness at life's end.

Psychology of death: Terror Management Theory shows that avoidance of death covertly distorts human behavior; the sūtra, by holding out "impermanence devours all" directly, replaces suppression with facing—confronting death's salience may bring, instead, a clarifying of values and composure.

Living Practice

Traditional: Relatives or fellow practitioners recite the Sūtra on Impermanence and chant the Buddha's name at the bedside, so the dying meet their final passage settled in mind and clear in mindfulness.

Modern: Speak of "death" with a loved one in advance—not waiting for crisis but, while healthy, candidly sharing each other's wishes for end-of-life care and farewell (an advance directive). This refusal to evade is the deepest "end-of-life care" you can give each other. You may also close each night with a minute of "recollecting death": aware that today has become the past, and tomorrow is not guaranteed.

Daily Cultivation

A minute of recollecting death: Before sleep tonight, quietly hold one line—"never has there been one single thing not devoured by impermanence." Not for dread but for clarity: if there were no tomorrow, what today should least be postponed? Let awareness of death become reverence for life.

Deeper Reflections

1. Vegetarianism was not the Buddha's original rule; Theravāda and Tibetan traditions still permit "threefold-pure meat," yet the Chinese tradition made it absolute. Is this a deepening of Mahāyāna compassion, or an accident of history and imperial power? On what should we ground it today?
Both are true. The deepening: Mahāyāna pushes the compassion of "not bearing to see them die" to its limit, and the meat-precept is a natural extension of that logic. The accident: abstention became an East Asian absolute partly through Emperor Wu of Liang's imperial enforcement—a historically contingent path to institutionalization. Today's steadier stance may be: rather than absolutizing vegetarianism in the name of "the Buddha's rule," ground it in the extension of compassion and ecological responsibility—respecting traditional differences while giving reasons that withstand modern scrutiny.
2. The Cakkavatti Sutta attributes violence to distributive failure—nearly "structural determinism." Where, then, do individual greed, hatred, and free choice stand? Does blaming structures cancel moral responsibility?
The sutta is in fact two-layered: structure (failing to give to the destitute) creates the soil for wrongdoing, but each act of theft or killing is still an individual creating karma, answerable to cause and effect. Buddhist dependent origination is precisely good at holding "many causes" without collapsing into single-factor determinism—structure is a condition, karma and choice are conditions, and together they form the whole. The lesson: improve structures to reduce the inducements to wrongdoing, while never absolving personal moral responsibility—speaking only of structure indulges, speaking only of the individual is merciless.
3. If "contentment with few desires" became mainstream, would it collide head-on with the growth logic on which the modern economy runs? Is Buddhism offering a sustainable path, or a romanticized refusal of development?
It does collide with the assumption of "infinite growth," but not necessarily with all development. What Buddhism questions is the equation of "more consumption" with "a better life," not the remedying of scarcity itself—it even demands "giving to the destitute." Read it as a "reorientation" of growth: from quantity toward quality and wellbeing, from possession toward experience and relationship. The risk is that it degenerate into the aloofness of the privileged—preaching "few desires" to those still in want is cruel; so "contentment" must first meet basic needs, and only then speak of transcending craving.
4. The Sūtra on Impermanence urges directness about death with the dying; yet many families choose to conceal the truth from a patient. Between candor and tenderness, can Buddhism's "seeing things as they are" itself become another cruelty?
The key is whether "as they are" is paired with the skillful means of compassion. Buddhism does not urge a cold verdict but "responsiveness to the person"—delivering the truth within the pace the other can bear and wishes to know, through gentle companionship. Concealment, though born of love, often robs the dying of their last chance to say farewell, complete their wishes, and settle the mind; yet forcing disclosure without regard for their state also violates compassion. The ideal is "facing it together": neither deceiving nor dropping the truth on someone alone—truth must be held in the vessel of presence and companionship.
