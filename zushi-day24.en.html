Chan · Hongzhou School (Nanyue line)

Record of Mazu Daoyi

Mazu Daoyi (709–788) · compiled by disciples · preserved in the Jingde Record of the Transmission of the Lamp, fasc. 28

Scripture Passage

「道不用修，但莫污染。何為污染？但有生死心、造作趣向，皆是污染。若欲直會其道，平常心是道。何謂平常心？無造作、無是非、無取捨、無斷常、無凡無聖……只如今行住坐臥、應機接物盡是道。」 Translation: The Way needs no cultivating—only do not defile it. What is defilement? Any mind of birth-and-death grasping, any contrived striving toward a goal, is defilement. To meet the Way directly: ordinary mind is the Way. What is ordinary mind? No contrivance, no right-and-wrong, no grasping-and-rejecting, no nihilism-or-eternalism, no ordinary-or-holy… Right now, walking, sitting, responding to circumstances and meeting things—all of it is the Way. (Jingde Record, discourse to the assembly)

Commentary

Mazu Daoyi, third generation below the Sixth Patriarch Huineng and heir to Nanyue Huairang, founded the Hongzhou school, from which over a hundred awakened disciples emerged. His record affirms with "this very mind is Buddha" and, to prevent clinging, negates with "not mind, not Buddha"—when asked why he taught the former, he replied "to stop a child's crying"; asked what to do once the crying stops, he said "not mind, not Buddha." Both affirming and negating are skillful means to stop students from chasing "Buddha" as something outside their own mind.

"Ordinary mind is the Way" is Mazu's deepest contribution: it pulls practice back from special meditative states and asceticism into the present activity of daily life. But "ordinary" is never license for laxity—it is the mind with all contrivance and grasping removed. The difficulty lies in "do not defile," not in "ordinary."

Cross-Disciplinary Resonance

With flow and embodied cognition: "No contrivance—responding to things is the Way" resonates strongly with flow in psychology. A master's skilled movements are precise precisely because they bypass deliberate central control; effortful self-monitoring degrades performance. "Ordinary mind" is close to that state of dropped self-monitoring and body-mind unity, but goes further: it does not depend on a match between skill and task difficulty—it is a transferable ground of mind.

Living Practice

Traditional: Hongzhou Chan made carrying water and gathering firewood the training hall; "ordinary mind" is honed in labor.

Modern: Pick one "boring" daily task—commuting, dishwashing, waiting for a build. Do it today without any goal-seeking mind of "hurry up / meanwhile think about work"—just walk, just wash. You'll find "ordinary mind" is not passivity but gathering scattered attention back onto this one thing at hand.

Daily Practice

"Do not defile" awareness: Today, each time you notice a striving mind arise over some small task ("should be faster / let me also do more"), silently say "ordinary mind" and return your attention to this one thing at hand. At night, count how many times you caught the contriving mind.
Chan · Linji (Rinzai) School

Record of Linji

Linji Yixuan (?–866) · compiled by Sansheng Huiran · late Tang

Scripture Passage

「赤肉團上有一無位真人，常從汝等諸人面門出入。未證據者，看看！」時有僧出問：「如何是無位真人？」師下禪床，把住云：「道！道！」其僧擬議，師托開云：「無位真人是什麼乾屎橛！」便歸方丈。 Translation: "On this lump of red flesh there is a true person of no rank, constantly going in and out through the faces (the six senses) of all of you. Those who have not yet witnessed it—look, look!" A monk stepped forward and asked, "What is the true person of no rank?" Linji got down from his seat, seized him: "Speak! Speak!" As the monk hesitated to speak, Linji shoved him away: "The true person of no rank—what a dried shit-stick!" and returned to his quarters. (Record of Linji, discourse)

Commentary

Linji Yixuan, heir to Huangbo Xiyun, founded the Linji school, the most flourishing of the Five Houses and the only lineage transmitted unbroken to today; his style was fierce, famed for "shouts and blows."

The "true person of no rank" points to the authentic nature everyone possesses, bound to no status or label. But Linji negates as fast as he affirms: the instant the monk "hesitated" (stirred a thought), he condemned it as a "dried shit-stick"—the moment you make the true person into an object to ask and answer about, it dies. "Kill the Buddha, kill the patriarch" (sever dependence on any authoritative concept) and "be master wherever you stand, and where you stand is true" express the same spirit: return spiritual sovereignty entirely to this living person, here and now, forbidding any outward seeking of idols.

Cross-Disciplinary Resonance

With the decentered nature of consciousness: "No rank"—no fixed location—echoes modern consciousness studies' rejection of the "Cartesian theater." Neuroscience and Dennett alike hold that there is no "little person" sitting in the center watching a screen; the self is an emergent, distributed process. Yet Linji's "true person" does not merely deconstruct the self—after deconstruction it remains fully present and able to act as master, a living dimension that no-self theory alone does not capture.

Living Practice

Traditional: Linji used shouts and blows to cut off students' habitual conceptualizing, forcing them to take up their true nature directly at the point where "hesitation" collapses.

Modern: When identity anxiety arises—"Am I a worthy super-individual?" "Does my title/output measure up?"—treat it as that monk's "hesitation." Ask: "This 'who' that is anxious—on what rank does it stand?" Anxiety attaches to identity labels, but the true person of no rank has no rank to lose; sovereignty returns to the person doing the work now.

Daily Practice

The "who is master" question: Today, when you feel pulled around by circumstances (dragged by meetings, notifications, others' judgments), pause one second and ask: "Right now, who is master?" Don't seek an answer—just raising the question of sovereignty loosens the pull of circumstance a notch.
Chan · Nanquan line (Mazu—Nanquan—Zhaozhou)

Record of Zhaozhou

Zhaozhou Congshen (778–897) · compiled by disciples · mid-to-late Tang

Scripture Passage

僧問：「狗子還有佛性也無？」州云：「無。」

師問新到：「曾到此間否？」云：「曾到。」師云：「喫茶去。」又問僧，僧云：「不曾到。」師云：「喫茶去。」院主問：「為什麼曾到也喫茶去，不曾到也喫茶去？」師喚院主，主應諾，師云：「喫茶去。」 Translation: A monk asked, "Does a dog have Buddha-nature?" Zhaozhou said, "No" (wu). (The Nirvana Sutra plainly states all beings have Buddha-nature; Zhaozhou answers "No" precisely to break the conceptual grasping.) Another: Zhaozhou asked a newcomer, "Have you been here before?" "Yes." "Go drink tea." He asked another; "No." "Go drink tea." The temple administrator asked why both received the same reply. Zhaozhou called his name; the man answered; Zhaozhou said, "Go drink tea." (Record of Zhaozhou)

Commentary

Zhaozhou Congshen, heir to Nanquan Puyuan (a disciple of Mazu), took up residence at Zhaozhou's Guanyin cloister only at eighty; famed for his sharp wit, he was honored as the "old Buddha of Zhaozhou."

"A dog has no Buddha-nature"—Zhaozhou's "wu"—later became the first huatou of Song-dynasty "critical-phrase Chan" (the first case of the Gateless Gate). It is not a philosophical judgment: to grasp "wu" as "does not have" is to miss it. It is a blade that severs the have/have-not dichotomy, forcing you to see your nature directly where concepts run out. "Go drink tea" is the other face of the same mind: whether you've been here or not, whether you understand or not, all alike—"go drink tea"—pulling the student from calculation back to this present cup. The fierce and the plain: both are Zhaozhou.

Cross-Disciplinary Resonance

With cognitive impasse and insight: Investigating "wu" deliberately manufactures a cognitive impasse that logic cannot dissolve—reason exhausted while the mind stays alive. This parallels cognitive science on "insight (aha)": breakthroughs often come just as reasoning hits a dead end and the existing predictive frame fails. The huatou gives no answer; it forces predictive coding to a collapse point where another kind of "seeing" can arise. But note carefully: the scientific aha is still problem-solving, whereas Chan awakening points to knowing itself—different levels, not to be simply equated.

Living Practice

Traditional: Critical-phrase Chan asks one to never leave the huatou throughout the day—walking, sitting, holding "a dog has no Buddha-nature—wu," keeping the sense of doubt unbroken.

Modern: Replace phone-scrolling in fragmented moments with "raising the huatou." In a queue, waiting for the elevator or for a model to finish, don't reach for the phone; just gently raise "wu"—not explaining it, only carrying the doubt of "what is this?" You'll experience attention shift from being pulled by the algorithm to being raised by yourself.

Daily Practice

A cup of tea, a huatou: Today, with your first cup of water or coffee, don't look at your phone or think—just drink. When done, raise Zhaozhou's "wu" with the doubt "what is this?" and pause for three breaths. Hold the presence of "go drink tea" and the doubt of "wu" together in one cup.
Chan · Linji patriarch (Huangbo Xiyun)

Huangbo: Essentials of the Transmission of Mind

Huangbo Xiyun (?–850) · recorded by chancellor Pei Xiu · c. 857

Scripture Passage

「諸佛與一切眾生，唯是一心，更無別法。此心無始已來，不曾生、不曾滅，不青不黃，無形無相，不屬有無，不計新舊，非長非短，非大非小，超過一切限量名言蹤跡對待，當體便是，動念即乖……唯此一心即是佛。」
「即心是佛，無心是道。」 Translation: All Buddhas and all sentient beings are only one mind, and there is no other dharma. This mind, since beginningless time, was never born and never dies; neither blue nor yellow, without form or shape, belonging neither to "being" nor "non-being," reckoned neither new nor old, neither long nor short, neither large nor small; it transcends all measure, names, traces, and opposition. The very substance is it; stir a thought and you miss it… Only this one mind is Buddha. "This very mind is Buddha; no-mind is the Way." (Essentials of Mind Transmission)

Commentary

Huangbo Xiyun, heir to Baizhang Huaihai and teacher of Linji Yixuan, carried on Mazu's Hongzhou line. The Essentials, recorded in the hand of chancellor Pei Xiu, is the most concentrated programmatic text of Chan's "mind-to-mind transmission."

The whole text reduces to one word: mind. Buddha and beings are not two; the only difference is delusion versus awakening. This mind is no thing, belongs neither to being nor non-being; "stir a thought and you miss it"—the moment you rouse a mind to seek or describe it, you have already turned away. So practice is not "attaining" anything but ceasing outward seeking. "This mind is Buddha" is the affirmative entry; "no-mind is the Way" is the ultimate resting place—"no-mind" is not blankness but the present mind that abides nowhere and raises no discrimination or grasping.

Cross-Disciplinary Resonance

With the "hard problem" of consciousness: Huangbo's "only one mind, no other dharma" is a thoroughgoing idealist position—taking mind (awareness) as the ground of all phenomena. This is in tune with idealism/panpsychism's challenge to physicalism in contemporary consciousness studies: why is there subjective experience at all (Chalmers' "hard problem")? Huangbo does not explain how mind is "produced"; he points directly to mind itself as ground. Here we must be honest: this is a metaphysical approach, not a scientific conclusion—the two can converse but cannot prove one another.

Living Practice

Traditional: Pei Xiu, though a chancellor, took the disciple's posture and investigated "this mind is Buddha" amid his official duties—proving this teaching does not depart from the world.

Modern: Apply "no-mind is the Way" to human-AI collaboration: practice not clinging to "this must be my idea." Release the attachment to "my output / my credit" (abide nowhere), and you'll actually see more clearly which proposal is truly best. "Ceasing outward seeking" is not inaction but removing that layer of self-aggrandizing attachment, leaving judgment clearer.

Daily Practice

A "non-abiding" release: Today, when making a decision or offering a proposal, first notice any attachment of "this had better be my credit / my being right." If it's there, try to set that layer down and ask only "which is truly better?" Feel that "no-mind" is not coldness but the clarity and lightness that remain once self-aggrandizement is dropped.

For Deeper Reflection

Dialogue between the records, differences of style, cross-disciplinary mapping, the edges of practice
1. Mazu's "ordinary mind is the Way" vs. Huangbo's "stir a thought and you miss it"—same mind, or in tension?
Superficially aligned, but with a division of labor. Mazu's "ordinary mind" falls on function—daily responding to things is the Way; Huangbo's "stir a thought and you miss it" falls on essence—rousing contrivance is deviation. The two are two faces of one thing. Take Mazu alone and "ordinary" slides into slack indulgence; take Huangbo alone and "no-thought" collapses into dead quietism. The Hongzhou-Linji line twists both together: in the most ordinary carrying of water and gathering of wood, sustain that ready-made mind undefiled by thought.
2. Zhaozhou answers "no" about the dog—does this contradict the Nirvana Sutra's "all beings have Buddha-nature"?
Doctrinally no contradiction; at the level of the turning-word he deliberately clashes. The sutra's "have" is a definitive affirmation—beings inherently possess the nature to become Buddha. Zhaozhou's "no" is a medicine that removes stickiness—if you come carrying "Buddha-nature is a thing that exists," I snatch away your "having." Zhaozhou is also recorded answering "yes" to other monks; answering yes or no is situational prescription, so "wu" is no philosophical thesis. Critical-phrase Chan took "wu" as huatou precisely because it clashes with the sutra's plain word, generating a doubt reason cannot reconcile. Clinging to "wu" as nihilism and clinging to "have" as a substance are two directions of the same disease.
3. Is Linji's "kill the Buddha" anti-authority and anti-tradition? How does it coexist with relying on a teacher?
What is "killed" is the idolized Buddha/patriarch concept in your mind, not the Dharma itself, still less an encouragement to contempt. Linji himself deeply honored his lineage (Huangbo struck him three times; Dayu broke him open). True respect is to personally realize what the teacher realized—not to depend on the teacher as an object of worship outside one's mind; dependence on idols is itself the deepest "being deluded by others." So "kill the Buddha" and "rely on a good teacher" coexist within one sequence: first honestly follow and study under a teacher; then, at a certain juncture, the teacher demands you set down even "the Buddha gained through the teacher," and only then do you truly stand on your own. Without the earlier reliance, "kill the Buddha" is merely a pretext for wild Chan.
4. Can "the huatou manufacturing a cognitive impasse" be equated with the scientific "aha moment"?
Structurally similar, but pointing differently—do not equate lightly. Similar: both break through when reasoning is at a dead end and the existing frame fails; the huatou does resemble forcing predictive coding to a collapse point. Two differences: first, the scientific aha is problem-solving, its object still an external puzzle; Chan awakening points to knowing itself—not solving a problem but seeing the mind that was solving. Second, after the aha the problem vanishes; after Chan awakening, practice truly begins. Equating them is another instance of the "structural similarity means ontological identity" slippage (just like the "quantum–dependent origination" misuse): they can inspire one another, but cannot prove one another.
5. All four patriarchs point to "the ready-made mind here and now"—so why still need shouts, blows, huatou, and all these devices?
Because "ready-made" is exactly the hardest to believe. Huangbo says "the very substance is it, stir a thought and you miss it"—the mind is ready-made, yet a student, on hearing this, immediately rouses a thought to "seek" the ready-made, and thereby drifts further. The entire function of the devices is to cut off this one thought of seeking: Mazu's "ordinary mind" dissolves your expectation of special states, Linji's shouts cut off hesitation, Zhaozhou's "wu" blocks the have/have-not discrimination, Huangbo's "no-mind" dismantles attachment. The devices add nothing; they only turn you from "seeking outward" back to "being present." Hence Chan's saying, "the moment you call it a thing you miss it"—every record is a finger pointing at the moon, and in the end even the finger must be dropped, or it becomes a new clinging.
