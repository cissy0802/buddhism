Āgama · The Origin of the Vinaya

The Gotamī Sutta (Madhyama Āgama)

Trans. Gautama Saṃghadeva, E. Jin dynasty · Madhyama Āgama, sutra 116

Scripture Passage

"Mahāprajāpatī Gautamī thrice petitioned the Buddha: May the World-Honored One permit women, in this true Dharma and Vinaya, to go forth in faith from the household life into homelessness, to train in the path."

"Ānanda! As one builds a dam upon a great lake so its waters shall not overflow, so do I now set forth for the bhikkhunīs eight rules of reverence, not to be crossed, not to be transgressed for as long as they live: a nun shall receive full ordination from the bhikkhu saṅgha; every fortnight she shall seek instruction from the monks; … even a nun of a hundred years' standing, seeing a newly ordained monk, shall bow at his feet with utmost humility." Mahāprajāpatī—the Buddha's aunt and foster mother—thrice implored the World-Honored One to let women go forth. He finally consented, but, as one dams a lake against flooding, laid down the "eight garudhammas," never to be transgressed for life: a nun must take full ordination from the monks' order, request instruction from monks each fortnight, and even a nun of a hundred years' standing must bow to a newly ordained monk.

Commentary

This records the ordination of the first bhikkhunī in Buddhist history—Mahāprajāpatī Gautamī. The Pali Cullavagga and the Chinese Vinayas share this episode. With it the nuns' order was established, and Buddhism became a fourfold assembly: monks, nuns, laymen, laywomen.

The controversy centers on the eight garudhammas, which institutionally place nuns below monks. Modern text-critical scholarship (Bhikkhu Anālayo, Shizuka Sasaki) notes that their phrasing conflicts with early Vinaya and is logically inverted—they presuppose an existing full nuns' order while claiming to condition its founding—and were likely a later, sectarian interpolation, not the Buddha's original decree.

More striking: the same sutta plainly affirms that women who go forth can attain all four fruits of the path (from stream-entry to arhatship). Institutional subordination and soteriological equality form the deepest tension in the Buddhist view of gender.

Cross-Disciplinary Resonance

Complexity science · path dependence: the garudhammas are a classic "frozen accident"—an expedient rule set at one historical moment (for the order's safety and social optics) that, once written into institution, self-perpetuates across generations, detached from its founding reason. In complex systems, early micro-choices lock in the entire downstream trajectory.

Distributed systems: like a legacy protocol—built for compatibility, then feared to touch years later though no one recalls why, because everything depends on it. "Institutional archaeology" means reading that comment in the source: "// don't touch, a hack from some year."

Practice in Life

Traditional: nuns keep the "dual-order ordination"—first from the nuns' order, then from the monks'—a procedure heavier than that of monks.

Modern: run an "institutional archaeology" in your team or home—pick a rule everyone obeys yet no one can explain (an approval flow, a house rule) and ask: does the problem it once solved still exist? Is it a dam, or mere inertia? The garudhammas teach us that distinguishing living precept from dead path dependence is the key to any organization's evolution.

Daily Practice

"Institutional archaeology" drill: this week pick one rule you follow but can't justify, and write down the "original problem" it aimed to solve. Does that problem still exist? If not, is it a dam or just inertia? Identify one "frozen accident" in your surroundings.
Pali Canon · Saṃyutta Nikāya

The Somā Sutta (Bhikkhunī Saṃyutta)

Theravāda · Saṃyutta Nikāya 5.2

Scripture Passage

Māra taunts the nun Somā: "The state the sages reach, so hard to attain—no woman with her two-finger wisdom can arrive there."

Somā replies: "What can womanhood do, when the mind is well-composed, when knowledge flows on, rightly seeing the Dhamma? Whoever thinks 'I am woman' or 'I am man' is bound by Māra, fit for him to address." Pali (Somā's verse): Itthibhāvo kiṃ kayirā, cittamhi susamāhite; ñāṇamhi vattamānamhi, sammā dhammaṃ vipassato.
"Two-finger wisdom" is a sexist idiom—women were said to test rice between two fingers, hence "shallow wit." Somā answers: when the mind is composed, wisdom present, phenomena rightly seen, what obstacle is "womanhood"? Whoever still thinks "I am woman" or "I am man" is precisely the one caught in Māra's grip.

Commentary

The Bhikkhunī Saṃyutta gathers ten short suttas of nuns defeating Māra—a rare female-centered chapter in the Pali canon. The Somā Sutta is the sharpest: facing Māra's belittling of women's intellect by "two-finger wisdom," Somā does not argue male-versus-female superiority but cuts the ground away—on the field of awakening, "male/female" is itself a deluded appearance, and to grasp it is to be bound.

It echoes the Therīgāthā: over seventy elder nuns recount their liberation in verse. Muttā sings, "Free, well free am I—free of three crooked things: the mortar, the pestle, and my hunchbacked husband," setting domestic bondage beside saṃsāric bondage, and attaining arhatship.

The doctrinal point: early Buddhism gives a flat "yes" to whether women can attain arhatship. Notions of "female impurity" and the "five obstructions" are later accretions shaped by Brahmanical society, not the root position of the Āgamas and Nikāyas.

Cross-Disciplinary Resonance

Cognitive neuroscience: the "two-finger wisdom" bias still echoes today. Neuroscience (Gina Rippon's critique of "neurosexism") shows that cognitive sex differences in the brain are systematically exaggerated, most stemming from social shaping rather than biology. Somā's reply is an empirical rebuttal two millennia early.

Non-self: "thinking 'I am woman/man' is Māra's bondage" points straight at a core insight of cognitive science—gender identity is but one of the brain's constructed "self-labels," not an intrinsic entity. Dissolving "I am woman/man" is the same act as dissolving self-grasping: the self is a narrative, gender only a variable within it.

Practice in Life

Traditional: nuns meet outer contempt with "one-pointedness of mind" (cittass'ekaggatā), resting without dispute.

Modern: next time your judgment is discounted for your identity (gender, age, the "mom" label), try a Somā-style response—no argument, no anger; first observe within: "is what arises now the self-grasping of 'I am slighted,' or clear awareness?" Move attention from "proving myself" back to "seeing things as they are," and the contempt loses purchase. This is not swallowing insult, but refusing to feed the delusion.

Daily Practice

"Don't identify with the label" drill: over a week, note three moments you're "labeled" (looked down on for gender or role). Each time, watch three breaths first, observe whether the arising "I" is grasping the sense of "being slighted," then respond calmly. Practice leaving contempt nothing to hold.
Lotus Section · King of Mahāyāna Sutras

The Dragon Girl's Buddhahood (Lotus Sutra, Devadatta chapter)

Trans. Kumārajīva, Later Qin · Lotus Sutra, fascicle 4, chapter 12

Scripture Passage

"Śāriputra said: '…Moreover a woman's body still has five obstructions: she cannot become a Brahmā-king, an Indra, a Māra-king, a wheel-turning monarch, or a Buddha. How could a female body swiftly attain buddhahood?'"

"At that moment the whole assembly saw the dragon girl suddenly transform into a male, complete the bodhisattva practices, and go at once to the Spotless World in the south, sit on a jeweled lotus, and attain perfect awakening." Śāriputra objects that a woman's body bears five obstructions—she cannot be Brahmā-king, Indra, Māra-king, wheel-turning monarch, or Buddha—so how attain buddhahood swiftly? Before he finishes, the eight-year-old dragon girl offers a jewel to the Buddha, who accepts it; she transforms then and there into a male, fulfills the bodhisattva path, and in the Spotless World attains full awakening upon a jeweled lotus.

Commentary

The dragon girl's buddhahood is the most famous—and most contested—gender case in the Mahāyāna. In an instant of offering a jewel, the eight-year-old awakens, refuting on the spot the doubt of Wisdom-Accumulation Bodhisattva and Śāriputra that "a female body cannot attain buddhahood."

The dispute lies in the "transformation into a male": she becomes male before awakening. One reading is capitulation—still assuming buddhahood requires a male form, conceding to the "five obstructions." Another is deconstruction—publicly demonstrating that "male/female" can convert in an instant, being illusory, thus proving gender unreal and no obstacle at all. Tiantai's Zhiyi read it as scriptural proof of "buddhahood in this very body," stressing the word "swift"; the change of sex is mere conventional display.

Compare the Vimalakīrti Sutra: a goddess uses her powers to turn Śāriputra into a woman and asks why he doesn't change back; he answers he doesn't know what to change into, and she declares, "all dharmas are neither male nor female"—negating the reality of gender even more thoroughly than the Lotus.

Cross-Disciplinary Resonance

Gender studies · essence vs. construction: the "become male" debate is an ancient rehearsal of today's "essentialism vs. constructionism." The goddess's "neither male nor female" is close to Judith Butler's "gender is performativity, not essence"—gender a mutable designation, not a fixed being.

Five obstructions as social conditioning: all five are worldly power-positions (Brahmā, Indra, wheel-turner)—a gendered monopoly of social roles, not a limit of the mind's capacity, as voices within Buddhism itself pointed out.

Practice in Life

Traditional: Mahāyāna practitioners contemplate "male and female as illusory," neither limiting themselves nor others by bodily form.

Modern: list three things you assume "a mom or a woman cannot or should not do" (a late-night business trip, being firm in a meeting, leaving the children for a retreat). Test them by the dragon girl's "suddenly": which are real conditional obstacles (to be negotiated), and which are merely "five obstructions" woven by habit and social script? Whatever can be broken, break it now.

Daily Practice

Testing the "five obstructions": write down one internalized "female/mom obstruction" (something you assume you can't do). This week do one small thing to test it—is it a real conditional obstacle, or a habit and social script you can break right now?
Tantra · Tibetan Tradition

The Twenty-One Praises of Ārya-Tārā

From the Root Tantra of All Tathāgatas' Mother · shared Tibetan-Chinese transmission

Scripture Passage

"Homage to Tārā, swift and heroic, whose eyes flash like an instant of lightning; born from the opening of a lotus, the lotus-face of the Protector of the three times."

"Homage to her within the Tathāgata's crown, supreme and unconquerable; who has perfected the boundless pāramitās, the closest refuge of all children of the Buddhas." Homage to Tārā—"swift" meaning quick to rescue. Her gaze is like lightning, her face like a lotus; she arises from the lotus born of the Buddha's tears of compassion, the refuge on whom the Buddhas of the three times rely; abiding in the Tathāgata's crown, she perfects every pāramitā, the highest refuge for all who practice.

Commentary

Tārā (Tib. Drolma) is the most widely revered female deity of Tibetan Buddhism, born from Avalokiteśvara's tears, savior from the "eight fears" (water, fire, lion, elephant, snake, thief, ruler, demon). Green Tārā signifies swift activity; White Tārā, longevity and the pacifying of calamity.

Tārā's cult carries a vital narrative: in the distant past, Princess Yeshe Dawa aroused the aspiration to buddhahood; when monks urged her to "vow to take a male body," she retorted, "here there is no man and no woman… those who grasp at man or woman are deluded fools," and vowed to practice the bodhisattva path and attain buddhahood in a female body, life after life, thus becoming Tārā. This is Buddhism's most positive, unreserved affirmation of female buddhahood—not by becoming male, but perfecting buddhahood in the female form itself.

Set beside the dragon girl (becoming male) and the goddess (neither male nor female), Tārā represents a third path—affirming the female form itself as the complete vehicle of ultimate awakening. Three sutras, three stances, together mapping the full spectrum of the Buddhist view of gender.

Cross-Disciplinary Resonance

Depth psychology: Tārā as a female deity of "compassion-and-wisdom united" resonates with Jung's anima (the repressed feminine archetype within) and the "Great Mother" image in the collective unconscious. To meditate on Tārā is, in psychological terms, to integrate the neglected capacities of acceptance, compassion, and intuition.

A caution: this holds only at the level of archetypal functional analogy, not a claim that Tārā is "merely" a psychological projection—Tibetan tradition regards her as a real manifestation of the dharma-body. The analogy stops here.

Practice in Life

Traditional: practitioners recite the Twenty-One Praises at dawn, hold the Green Tārā mantra "oṃ tāre tuttāre ture svāhā," and recall Tārā in moments of fear.

Modern: Tārā's core quality is being "swift and heroic"—acting decisively in the very moment of fear. Next time a high-pressure decision brings timidity (a negotiation, a conflict, a child's emergency), recite the Tārā mantra three times and take her "swift-to-rescue" momentum: not suppressing fear, but starting compassion and action together. Tārā's practice is exactly "afraid, and still moving."

Daily Practice

"Afraid, and still moving" drill: set a "Tārā cue-phrase" for the week. Whenever fear makes you delay, recite it three times, take its "swift and heroic" momentum, and start action and compassion together—rather than waiting for fear to pass.

Questions to Deepen

1. If the garudhammas are indeed a later addition, should today's nuns' orders abolish them? When "fidelity to the Buddha's rule" conflicts with "fidelity to the Buddha's intent (equality in awakening)," which comes first?
This is a dispute between the letter and the spirit of the Vinaya. Conservatives hold that not a word of the precepts may change, lest the precept-essence be lost; reformers hold that a layered addition is not the Buddha's own heart and should return to the root intent of equal awakening. The answer depends on whether you take "the Buddha's rule" as a fixed set of clauses or as a living intent pointing toward liberation.
2. Dragon girl ("become male"), goddess ("neither male nor female"), Tārā ("buddhahood in the female form")—which stance is more ultimate, or do they suit different capacities?
The three form a deepening spectrum. The dragon girl still assumes the male form, a concession to the society of the time; the goddess wholly negates the reality of gender, meeting the ultimate directly; Tārā, on that ground of ultimate non-difference, further affirms the completeness of the female form at the conventional level. In view, the goddess is most thoroughgoing; in real-world healing and encouragement, Tārā is most powerful. Ultimate and expedient need not be ranked.
3. The Theravāda bhikkhunī lineage, once broken, has been revived in modern times (in 2007 Chinese-tradition monastics helped ordain Theravāda nuns), which some elders denounce as "invalid." Which weighs more, "unbroken bloodline" or "revival of spirit"?
The crux is how the precept-lineage's legitimacy is defined. Conservatives stress that the ordination-transmission cannot be broken—once severed, it cannot be regenerated; reformers hold it can be restarted through the surviving lineage of a same-Vinaya school (Chinese tradition). This is where the three great traditions differ most: the Chinese lineage never broke, the Tibetan is being restored, the Theravāda still debates. It presses the question: does religious legitimacy rest on continuity of form, or truth of spirit?
4. Is transposing Buddhism's "gender is illusory" directly onto modern gender politics a deepening or a misuse? Can "no male or female ultimately" evade the real responsibility of "inequality conventionally"?
It is easily misused. To pull out "no male or female ultimately" as a way to fob off structural inequality ("since it's illusory, why fight for equality") is to take world-transcending wisdom as worldly escape—precisely confusing the two truths. Candrakīrti long warned that the ultimate does not negate conventional causality but guarantees it. The true middle way: at the ultimate, know gender is illusory and grasp it not; at the conventional, still shoulder in full the duty of ending inequality. Emptiness is never an excuse for inaction, but a selfless, more thorough way of acting.
