Chinese Repentance Liturgy · Phenomenal Rite

The Cibei Daochang Repentance (Emperor Liang's Repentance)

Compiled by Baozhi et al. at imperial command · 6th c. · 10 fascicles, 40 chapters

Scripture Passage

"Repentance is at root the reforming of the past and the cultivating of the future—ending evil, raising good. Living in this world, who can be without fault? Even a trainee-sage, losing mindfulness, still gives rise to afflictions; even an arhat, with residual habit, still stirs deeds of body and speech. How much more the ordinary person—how could there be no fault?" — Fascicle 1, Chapter on Repentance (Third)

Commentary

This rite is traditionally said to have been made for Emperor Wu of Liang's consort, Lady Chi—reborn (the legend says) as a serpent through jealous venom. The emperor commanded Baozhi and other masters to compile scriptural passages into a repentance; after its performance she was released. The name "Compassion Sanctuary" was received in a dream of Maitreya. The whole rite unfolds through refuge, resolving doubt, repentance, arousing the mind, vowing, and dedication, and remains the most widely practiced repentance in Chinese Buddhism.

Its frame rests on two senses: chan—openly disclosing past faults, not concealing them; and hui—vowing to cut off recurrence. This is no prayer for pardon; buddhas grant no absolution. Rather, through disclosure the mind's grip on hidden guilt loosens, and through vowing it turns toward good. This phenomenal repentance (ritual disclosure) must run paired with principle-repentance (seeing the empty nature of sin), or it lapses into empty form.

Cross-Disciplinary Resonance

Clinical psychology: "Disclosure, not concealment" echoes the healing mechanism of exposure and confession—voicing or writing what shames us lowers the amygdala's threat response and lets memory re-integrate. Concealment (suppression) instead sustains the loop of distress.

Moral repair: "Reform the past" differs from mere guilt. Psychology distinguishes guilt ("I did a wrong thing," reparable) from shame ("I am bad," paralyzing)—and the repentance text always lands on the action of reform, never on self-condemnation.

Living Practice

Traditional setting: Monasteries perform Emperor Liang's Repentance over three to seven days at year's end or in memorial rites, prostrating and disclosing chapter by chapter.

Modern application: After losing your temper with a child or colleague, don't idle in "I'm terrible." Do a micro-repentance: write concretely—what I did (disclose), who was hurt (fruit), how I'll respond to the same trigger next time (reform). Three lines turn rumination into a workable correction.

Daily Practice

Three-line disclosure: Before sleep, on one thing you regret today, write three lines—(1) what I did, stated without judgment; (2) who was affected; (3) what I'll do in the same situation tomorrow. Then set it down; don't ruminate.
Tiantai · Principle-Repentance

The Lotus Samadhi Repentance Rite

By Zhiyi · 6th c. · Based on the Lotus Sutra & the Samantabhadra Contemplation Sutra

Scripture Passage

"The whole ocean of karmic obstruction arises from deluded thought. If you would repent, sit upright and contemplate ultimate reality. The mass of sins is like frost and dew—the sun of wisdom can dissolve it away." — Sutra on the Contemplation of Samantabhadra, the basis of the Lotus repentance

Commentary

Drawing on the Lotus Sutra's "Encouragement of Samantabhadra" and the Contemplation Sutra, Zhiyi established a twenty-one-day rite, one of Tiantai's four samadhis. It drives repentance from "phenomena" toward "principle": the early stages still prostrate, chant, and disclose (phenomenal repentance), but the heart lies in the closing "sit upright and contemplate reality"—seeing that the nature of sin is empty, unborn and formless, so that no sin can be found and no one repents. This is principle-repentance (the repentance of non-arising).

The rite includes the Five Repentances: repent, request (that buddhas remain and teach), rejoice (in others' good), dedicate, and vow; plus the six-sense-faculty confession—disclosing in turn the defilement gathered by eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, mind. Phenomenal repentance cleanses the coarse; principle-repentance uproots the source. Contemplate no reality and sin returns; grasp emptiness while abandoning practice and one grows arrogant and shameless. The two are a bird's two wings.

Cross-Disciplinary Resonance

Cognitive therapy: "Contemplate reality" is close to CBT's cognitive defusion—not identifying with a thought, stepping back to see "this is a thought" rather than "this is me / this is fact." The thought drops from "absolute truth" to "a phenomenon arising and passing in mind"—a modern echo of "all arises from deluded thought."

Metacognition: Sitting and watching sin-images rise and fall trains metacognitive monitoring—not entering the content, only observing the mind's own operation, mirroring mindfulness research on prefrontal regulation of the limbic system.

Living Practice

Traditional setting: Tiantai practitioners keep a twenty-one-day retreat, circumambulating and chanting the Lotus at the six periods of day and night while contemplating reality, to realize the Lotus Samadhi.

Modern application: When a thought keeps gnawing ("that remark was wrong, that decision failed"), first do phenomenal repentance (admit, apologize, repair), then a minute of principle-repentance: sit, watch the self-blaming thought, silently note "it arises from delusion," and watch it rise, abide, pass—without suppressing or following. You'll find the sin, like frost, thins under the sun.

Daily Practice

Frost-and-dew contemplation (1 min): When a self-blaming thought arises, watch it for three breaths: Where is it now? Does it have color or shape? Watch it self-arise and self-vanish. Silently note "sins are like frost and dew," and don't extend the thought.
Avatamsaka · Vows & Conduct

The Practices and Vows of Samantabhadra

Trans. Prajña (Zhenyuan recension) · late 8th c. · Avatamsaka Sutra, "Entering the Inconceivable" chapter

Scripture Passage

"Whatever evil deeds I have done of old, all born of beginningless greed, hatred, and delusion, arising from body, speech, and mind—all of them I now fully repent." — The Ten Great Vows, "Repenting Karmic Obstacles"

Commentary

This chapter caps the Avatamsaka Sutra, where Samantabhadra gathers all practice into Ten Great Vows: revere all buddhas, praise the Tathagatas, make offerings, repent karmic obstacles, rejoice in merit, request the turning of the Dharma-wheel, request the buddhas to remain, always follow the Buddha's example, forever accord with beings, and universally dedicate. Repentance here is not a standalone method but one link in the ten—followed at once by rejoicing, dedicating, vowing—so wrongdoing is converted into the provisions of awakening.

Its grandeur is inexhaustibility: each vow proceeds "thought after thought without interruption, body-speech-mind without weariness"; though space, beings, and afflictions be exhausted, my vow is never exhausted. It elevates a one-time repentance into a vow-momentum spanning all future time, and ends in rebirth in the Pure Land—joining hands with the Pure Land path.

Cross-Disciplinary Resonance

Science of intention and habit: "Thought after thought without interruption" fits research on implementation intentions and habit loops—a one-off resolve decays; only a vow repeatedly renewed and bound to context becomes automatic behavior. The vows are a compound interest of intention "across all future time."

A systems view of altruism: "Forever accord with beings" and "universally dedicate" rewrite good deeds from isolated events into continuous output aimed at the whole web of relations—closer to embedding individual acts into system-level positive feedback than to tallying single gains and losses.

Living Practice

Traditional setting: Chinese monasteries chant this chapter's prose or verses at evening service, gathering the mind and dedicating through the ten vows daily.

Modern application: Take "repent obstacles, then rejoice in merit" as a mind-method: whenever you fret over a mistake, immediately find one thing someone (or you) did well today, and sincerely rejoice. Repentance clears the negative; rejoicing stores the positive—two steps, one turn, so emotion flows toward building rather than collapsing into guilt.

Daily Practice

Repent-and-rejoice pivot: Each time today you fret over a fault, immediately recall one good worth rejoicing in (a colleague's patience, a child's honesty, your own moment of restraint), and sincerely say "I rejoice." Let every self-blame draw out one turn toward the good.
Chinese Repentance Liturgy · Three Obstacles

The Samadhi Water Repentance

By Zhixuan (Master Wuda) · 9th c. · After Zongmi's Rite of Cultivation of the Perfect Enlightenment Sutra · 3 fascicles

Scripture Passage

"Because beings' defilement is heavy—who is without sin? Who is without fault? Ordinary beings act in folly, covered by the dark of ignorance, drawing near bad friends, their minds disordered by affliction. … First, afflictions; second, action; third, retribution. These three can obstruct the sagely path and every excellent good of humans and gods—so the sutras name them the Three Obstacles." — Fascicle One

Commentary

This rite springs from a famous koan: Master Wuda grew a human-faced sore on his knee, in agony. The sage Kanaka washed it with the water of samadhi, and the sore spoke—it was a grudge from ten lifetimes past, from Yuan Ang's execution of Chao Cuo; because Wuda had been an eminent, precept-adorned monk for ten lives, the grudge found no opening, and only now ripened. Wuda then composed this repentance, likening samadhi-water to what washes the mind's grime—hence "Water Repentance."

Its program is to repent the three obstacles in turn: afflictions (greed, hatred, delusion), action (deeds of body-speech-mind driven by them), and retribution (the bitter fruit those deeds ripen into). The three breed one another—delusion drives action, action ripens into suffering, and undergoing suffering breeds more delusion. The rite teaches one to trace backward from the coarsest "fruit," disclosing layer by layer down to the subtlest "affliction," severing the loop. It unites phenomena and principle: ritual disclosure carried by the insight that "sin arises from mind, so repent with mind."

Cross-Disciplinary Resonance

Mind-body medicine: The "human-faced sore" is a metaphor—unresolved resentment and shame can surface in somatic form (chronic pain, immune dysregulation). Mind-body medicine confirms that sustained hostility and guilt affect cortisol and inflammation; "washing the mind" to heal is an ancient image of psychological intervention altering physiology (analogy, not causal claim).

The loop-structure of the three obstacles: affliction → action → retribution → affliction is a self-reinforcing feedback loop; in complex systems, breaking a vicious cycle often requires intervening at several nodes at once—just as the Water Repentance addresses not only the fruit but traces back to the root affliction, the opposite of merely treating symptoms.

Living Practice

Traditional setting: Monasteries perform the three fascicles of the Water Repentance to avert calamity, bless, and release grudges between "foe and kin," with special weight on "dissolving grudges and untying knots."

Modern application: For a relationship conflict that keeps draining you, review it by "tracing back the three obstacles": look first at the retribution (the present painful result) → then the action (what words or deeds of mine pushed it) → finally the affliction (what underlying attachment drives it: fear of losing control? needing to be right?). Loosen an inch at that deepest layer, and the whole chain truly releases—rather than reprocessing surface emotion again and again.

Daily Practice

Tracing the three obstacles (10 min): Pick a conflict that recently troubled you repeatedly. Write in turn: retribution = the bitter result now → action = which of my words/deeds pushed it → affliction = what attachment underneath drives me. At the last layer, write one sentence that loosens it.

Going Deeper

Dialogue across texts, tradition-differences, cross-disciplinary maps, the edges of practice
1. If "buddhas grant no pardon," what does repentance actually change?
It changes the sinning mind and its continuation, not a ledger wiped clean. The law of karma operates as it does; a buddha has neither power nor wish to "absolve." Repentance works two ways: disclosure loosens the concealed knot and stops the further evil of "hiding"; and repentance deepened to principle—seeing the empty nature of sin ("sin arises from mind, so repent with mind; when the mind is stilled, sin too is gone")—uproots the grasping that drives retribution. So repentance is not a transaction but a turning of the mind: heavy karma received lightly, the conditions of fixed karma shifted—yet nothing vanishes without cause. Treating repentance as an "indulgence" is exactly the grasping it means to break.
2. What happens if you take only phenomenal or only principle repentance?
Each falls to one extreme. Phenomena without principle: endless prostration and disclosure, but the root (affliction) untouched—so sin returns, breeding a subtle conceit of "how diligent I am," lapsing into form. Principle without phenomena: reciting "sin's nature is empty" while neither bowing nor making amends is using emptiness-view as a shield for indulgence—the Chan schools' rebuke of "denying cause and effect," shameless. Zhiyi's Lotus repentance runs both: the early phenomenal stages cleanse coarse traces, the closing "contemplate reality" uproots the fine. What is emptied is sin's substantiality, not the functional reality of cause and effect—the same frame as Madhyamaka's "non-duality of the two truths" (Day 2).
3. Does the Water Repentance's "ten-lifetime grudge" agree with Theravada's view of karma?
Broadly yes (self-done, self-received; karma is not lost), with differences in detail—no need to force a match. Theravada Abhidhamma classes karma as fixed/indefinite and heavy/light, with fruit across three times, but rarely elaborates multi-life narratives of a specific named grudge, weighting instead the moment-to-moment operation of karma in each mind-instant. The "Yuan Ang / Chao Cuo" koan is a skillful narrative carrying the principle of karmic retribution, meant to spur shame and the wish to untie grudges, not as historical documentation. Take its practice-structure—"trace back the three obstacles, break the loop"—for real benefit; grasp the plot as literal history and you miss its point. See tradition-differences as they are, without exalting one over the other.
4. Can psychology's "self-forgiveness" be equated with Buddhist repentance?
There is overlap, but not identity. Shared: both reject paralyzing shame-punishment and point to "acknowledge—repair—move on." Different in two ways: first, in self-forgiveness the subject and object are both "I," operating on the conventional psychological plane; Buddhist repentance goes further, toward no-self—in principle-repentance even "the I who repents" is contemplated as empty, not soothing the self but seeing through it. Second, repentance is always continuous with vowing and dedication (as in the ten Samantabhadra vows), joining the negative clearing to a positive, being-directed action, not stopping at personal emotional relief. Let them illuminate each other; don't reduce one to the other.
5. Can vowing become another form of attachment? How do vow and craving differ?
The key is object and attitude, not "wanting" itself. Craving is conditioned on self, sticks to results, frets over gain and loss—elated by success, vexed by failure. A vow is conditioned on awakening and beings, weights the cultivation of causes, and proceeds "thought after thought without weariness" yet without anxiety. Samantabhadra's vows close with "universally dedicate," precisely the checkpoint that keeps vow from turning to craving—vow-power is credited not to "my achievement" but dedicated to the whole dharma-realm, leaving self-grasping nowhere to land. So the highest vow is selfless, inexhaustible, and able to release the result; the moment you start reckoning "I made a vow, why hasn't it worked," craving has crept in and must be cleansed by repentance again. Vow and repentance are two motions of one body.
