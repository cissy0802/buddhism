Chan · The Sixth Patriarch's Treasure

Platform Sutra, Prajñā Chapter: "Dharma Within the World"

Spoken by Huineng · Compiled by Fahai · Ming-era Zongbao edition · Formless Verse

Scripture Passage

"The Buddhadharma is within the world; awakening is not apart from the world. To seek bodhi apart from the world is like searching for a rabbit's horns."

"Right view is called world-transcending; wrong view is called worldly. When both wrong and right are struck away, the nature of bodhi stands vividly revealed." Awakening lies right here in the human world and cannot be sought apart from it; to abandon the world and hunt for bodhi elsewhere is to look for horns on a rabbit—there are none. So-called "transcendence" is right view arising in the mind; the "worldly" is falling into wrong view. Strike away clinging to both wrong and right, and the intrinsically pure awakened nature reveals itself at once.

Commentary

These four lines of the Formless Verse are the single most-cited scriptural basis of the twentieth-century Humanistic Buddhism movement. Reversing the popular notion of "renouncing the world to seek a pure land elsewhere," Huineng nails awakening firmly to the ground of "the world": bodhi is not beyond the mountains and clouds, but right here, in this moment among people and affairs.

Everything hinges on "not apart." It does not urge attachment to the world, but insists the site of awakening can only be the world—apart from affliction there is no place to polish bodhi, just as apart from waves there is no seeing water. Taixu, Yinshun, and Hsing Yun all trace their Humanistic Buddhism back to this one line.

"Strike away both wrong and right" goes further: even the thought "I must transcend the world" is a clinging. True freedom is to act within the world yet not be bound by its gains and losses—the most practical key Chan hands the modern person.

Cross-Disciplinary Resonance

With embodied cognition: "Awakening not apart from the world" resonates with the core claim of embodied cognition—mind is not disembodied computation but arises only through the body's interaction with its environment. Awakening does not happen in a vacuum "apart from the world," just as cognition does not happen in a bodiless brain. Clinging to either pole—"transcendent" or "worldly"—mistakes an emergence-in-relation for an isolated entity.

Living Practice

Traditional context: Chan holds that "carrying water and chopping wood are none other than the wondrous Way"—the practitioner investigates within daily chores, not waiting to enter meditative absorption to seek elsewhere.

Modern application: Treat your desk, meetings, and childcare as your training ground, not obstacles to practice. Next time you think "I'll practice once I'm through this rush, once the kids are older," let Huineng remind you: that "pure land later" is precisely the rabbit's horns. The chance to awaken is only in the very task in your hands now.

Daily Cultivation

"This moment is the training ground": Today pick the chore you most want to escape (email, coaxing a child to sleep, waiting in line), and as you do it silently recite "awakening not apart from the world." Don't treat it as practice's enemy but as the only real site—watch the moment resistance loosens.
Modern · Buddhist Reform

Taixu: "How to Build Humanistic Buddhism"

Master Taixu (1890–1947) · Lecture, 1933 · with the "Buddhahood-through-personhood" verse

Scripture Passage

"Humanistic Buddhism means Buddhism that does not teach people to leave humanity to become gods or ghosts… but rather uses Buddhist principles to improve society, advance humankind, and better the world."

"We look up only to the Buddha, fulfilled in completed character; when the person is complete, buddhahood is complete—this is what is truly real." Humanistic Buddhism clarifies that Buddhism is not about leaving the human crowd to become gods or ghosts, nor about everyone fleeing into mountain monasteries to become monks; rather, it uses the truths of the Dharma to improve society, advance humankind, and make this world better. (Verse) The highest standard we look up to is only the Buddha, and that attainment is realized in the completion of character—when personhood is perfected, buddhahood is complete; this is the truly real teaching.

Commentary

Taixu is the founder of "Humanistic Buddhism" (his early term was "Buddhism for human life," later merged with "humanistic"). Facing a late-Qing/early-Republic Buddhism derided as "funeral rites, gods and ghosts, and world-fleeing recluses," he proposed the church-shaking "three revolutions": of doctrine (break superstition, center human life), of institution (reform the monastic order), and of property (turn temple assets to education and charity).

"When the person is complete, buddhahood is complete" distills his thought: becoming a buddha requires no abandonment of being human—it is completed precisely in perfecting one's personhood. This pulls the distant fruit of buddhahood back into each person's present cultivation of character, a modern restatement of Huineng's "Dharma within the world."

Taixu's institutional reforms largely failed in his lifetime (the conservative establishment was vast), but the ideas had been sown—his student Yinshun and follower Hsing Yun would later grow that seed into a great tree.

Cross-Disciplinary Resonance

With organizational change: The "three revolutions" were a classic top-down institutional redesign, and their failure confirms an insight of organization theory—reform that changes only ideas (software) but cannot move entrenched interests in property and headcount (hardware) is easily devoured by path dependence. Taixu failed at property and institution, the two "hardest" layers.

With leadership: Taixu illustrates the gap between "visionary leader" and "execution": a great idea needs an organizer to inherit the baton (Hsing Yun) before it can scale. Vision and institution-building are two different talents.

Living Practice

Traditional context: Taixu founded the Wuchang Buddhist Institute and the Sino-Tibetan Buddhist Institute, training clergy through a modern curriculum in place of the traditional master-disciple transmission.

Modern application: For anyone pursuing the "super-individual," Taixu offers a caution: however lofty the goal, it must rest on the foundation of "completed personhood." Don't suspend "becoming an AI super-individual" as an external myth—first ask whether, in honesty, patience, and responsibility, you have become one measure more "a complete person" today. Person complete, then work complete.

Daily Cultivation

"Person complete = buddhahood complete" reflection: Tonight, instead of asking "how far am I from my goal," ask a Taixu-style question—"as a 'person,' in what one point am I more complete than yesterday?" (a kept promise, a yielded point, a shouldered duty). Ground the grand goal in a single day's gain in character.
Modern · Theory of Humanistic Buddhism

Yinshun: "The Buddha in the Human Realm"

Master Yinshun (1906–2005) · collected 1951 · argued from the Ekottara Āgama

Scripture Passage

Citing the Ekottara Āgama: "All Buddhas, the World-Honored Ones, appear in the human realm; none attains buddhahood in the heavens."

Yinshun: "Just as the Buddha became a buddha from being human, so should we, from being human, turn toward the buddha-path." (The Buddha in the Human Realm) Yinshun cites the Āgama's verdict—every Buddha awakened in the "human realm," none in the heavens—and argues: since the Buddha practiced and awakened in the status of a "human," we too should tread the human path toward buddhahood, with no need to abandon the human for the heavenly, or this world for another.

Commentary

Yinshun, Taixu's student, is Humanistic Buddhism's most rigorous theoretical architect. With deep textual scholarship he unearthed from the Āgamas the root proof-text "all Buddhas appear in the human realm," giving the movement an unassailable canonical basis—no longer merely a responsive slogan.

He proposed the famous principle "according with truth and according with capacity" (qìlǐ qìjī): propagation must both accord with truth (not violate the right Dharma of dependent-arising and emptiness) and accord with the age's capacities (answer the modern condition). Lacking either—truth alone stays too lofty for anyone to hear; capacity alone panders and loses authenticity.

He further used this to appraise Buddhist history, criticizing the later "heavenization and deification" trend (worshiping buddhas and bodhisattvas as fortune-granting gods) and urging return to the Buddha's original "human" character—a sharp stance that sparked lasting debate within the tradition and is a key coordinate for understanding modern Chinese Buddhism.

Cross-Disciplinary Resonance

With textual scholarship: Yinshun's method—proving meaning by scripture, layer by layer collating the Āgamas against later Mahāyāna—is structurally akin to historical philology's "return to the earliest textual stratum": strip away later accretions to approach the original form of the teaching. It is a kind of Buddhism-internal "archaeology."

With the communication of ideas: "Truth and capacity" maps precisely onto the dual constraint of any transmission of thought—the truth-value of content (truth) and fit to the audience (capacity). A model (an AI included) satisfying only one is either correct but unheard, or popular but distorted.

Living Practice

Traditional context: Yinshun's lifetime output approached seven million words; "learning for the sake of the Dharma, for the sake of Buddhism," he modeled "scholarship as practice."

Modern application: Take "truth and capacity" as the yardstick for your own expression and collaboration. Next time you write a proposal, teach a child, or lead a team, ask twice: Is this "correct" (truth)? Can this person, at this moment, take it in (capacity)? Only-correct slides into preaching; only-agreeable loses principle—holding both is real communication.

Daily Cultivation

"Truth and capacity" double-check: This week, before each important exchange, take ten seconds for two questions—(1) Is what I mean true and correct? (2) Can the other person, in their present situation, hear it? Note one time you hit a wall by attending to only one, and next time add the missing question.
Contemporary · Humanistic Buddhism in Practice

Hsing Yun: "A Blueprint for Humanistic Buddhism"

Master Hsing Yun (1927–2023) · Founder of Fo Guang Shan · with its Four Objectives

Scripture Passage

"What the Buddha taught, what people need, what purifies, what is wholesome and good; whatever helps toward a happy life is Humanistic Buddhism."

Fo Guang Shan's Four Objectives: "Propagate the Dharma through culture, nurture talent through education, benefit society through charity, and purify hearts through communal cultivation." Hsing Yun's definition of Humanistic Buddhism gives four measures—it is what the Buddha taught (not against right Dharma), what people need (fitting human life), what purifies (leading to clarity), and what is wholesome and good; whatever meets these four and helps achieve a happy life is Humanistic Buddhism. On this basis Fo Guang Shan set its four objectives: propagate through culture, nurture through education, relieve through charity, purify through communal cultivation.

Commentary

If Taixu was the idea and Yinshun the theory, Hsing Yun was the practitioner at scale. Founding Fo Guang Shan in 1967, he turned Humanistic Buddhism from books into a global propagation system spanning five continents—universities, art museums, a television station, free clinics, and disaster relief.

The four phrases "what the Buddha taught, what people need, what purifies, what is wholesome and good" are the plainest yet most precise definition of Humanistic Buddhism—translating profound doctrine into the everyday language of happiness people can feel, the very acme of "according with capacity." The Four Objectives then break propagation into four operable, institutionalized channels: culture, education, charity, communal cultivation.

Hsing Yun also faced the controversy head-on: with an organization this vast, how does one avoid the worldliness of "running religion as a business"? His answer was "With a transcendent spirit, do the world's work"—handling tangible, formed affairs with a selfless, altruistic heart. This line is the landing point of all four texts.

Cross-Disciplinary Resonance

With distributed organizations: Fo Guang Shan's worldwide network of temples resembles a "distributed system"—a unified set of objectives and teachings as a shared protocol, while local branch temples self-govern and adapt to local conditions, holding scaled consistency through values rather than pure hierarchical command.

With platform thinking: Splitting the Dharma into four "interfaces"—culture, education, charity, communal cultivation—lets people of different dispositions enter through different doors, lowering the threshold and widening reach: a kind of "platform thinking."

Living Practice

Traditional context: Fo Guang members practice the "Three Acts of Goodness" (do good deeds, speak good words, keep a good heart) and the "Four Givings" (give others confidence, joy, hope, and convenience), landing practice in everyday dealings.

Modern application: Take "with a transcendent spirit, do the world's work" as your work ethos: do the work all-out, refining it to excellence (the world's work), while the intention is altruistic and selfless, not for private name (the transcendent spirit). Next time you take on a big project, first calibrate the intention slot—do the work beautifully, yet let the heart not be bound by success or failure.

Daily Cultivation

"Transcendent spirit, worldly work" calibration: This week, before an important task, self-check in two slots—(1) The work: will I do it to the best I can? (2) The heart: is my intention altruistic, or only for my own name and outcome? Calibrate the heart-slot to selflessness, then throw yourself fully in.

Deeper Reflections

1. Taixu's "three revolutions" largely failed in his lifetime, yet Yinshun's and Hsing Yun's Humanistic Buddhism flourished—was the idea simply awaiting its season, or did Taixu fail for lack of an organizer's hand?
Both. Ideas need historical soil: Republican upheaval and coveted temple property made reform hardest; postwar Taiwan's stability and social need gave Yinshun room to write and Hsing Yun room to build. But there was also a difference of gifts—Taixu was a prophetic visionary, Hsing Yun a builder of institutions. Great ideas often need two generations relaying: one lights the fire, the other builds the furnace.
2. Humanistic Buddhism stresses worldly engagement, charity, and a happy life; critics fear it is "too human"—diluting renunciation, nirvāṇa, and liberation from birth-and-death. Is this deepening the Dharma, or thinning it?
This is Humanistic Buddhism's most central debate. Sympathizers say: Huineng long ago taught "Dharma within the world"; engagement is the Mahāyāna's very intent, and transcendent liberation is accomplished precisely in the trials of engagement. The worried say: if only social concern remains without the urgency of liberation, Buddhism becomes indistinguishable from a charity. The key may lie in "with a transcendent spirit, do the world's work"—engagement is the act, transcendence the heart; drop either and it tilts.
3. Fo Guang Shan and Tzu Chi have become transnational mega-organizations, holding assets, running media, and touching public affairs. Is the scaling and institutionalization of religion a boon to propagation, or the beginning of worldliness?
Scale is double-edged. The good: wide reach, deep resources, the capacity for great works (relief, education). The risk: once an organization grows vast, self-perpetuation and expansion can quietly displace the original propagation-intent—Taixu's feared "property" problem returning in new form. The defense lies not in refusing scale but in governance: whether the institution stays subordinate to its objectives, the "enterprise" ever checked by the "spirit." This is the perennial exam of every ideal-type organization, enterprises included.
4. Yinshun's "truth and capacity" requires propagation to fit the age's capacities. So, facing the AI era, scientific rationality, and global secularization, how far should Humanistic Buddhism update its "capacity" without losing its "truth"?
This hands the burden back to the present. Capacity requires the Dharma to converse with neuroscience, psychology, and AI ethics, in language moderns can understand; truth draws the line—the root meanings of dependent-arising, non-self, and compassion cannot be ceded to please. Two dangers: conservatism so rigid it grows sealed off from the age (losing capacity), or pandering until only spiritual chicken-soup and a mindfulness industry remain (losing truth). True modernization keeps the ancient right Dharma sharp in the new context, rather than blunting it.
