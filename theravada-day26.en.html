Theravada · Master Treatise

The Path of Purification (Visuddhimagga)

By Buddhaghosa · c. 5th century CE · Mahāvihāra tradition, Sri Lanka

Scripture

"Sīle patiṭṭhāya naro sapañño, cittaṃ paññañca bhāvayaṃ; ātāpī nipako bhikkhu, so imaṃ vijaṭaye jaṭaṃ." Trans.: A wise person established in virtue, developing mind and wisdom—an ardent, prudent monk—can disentangle this tangle. This verse (from the Saṃyutta Nikāya) is the skeleton of the whole work: "virtue—mind (concentration)—wisdom" names its three great parts.

Commentary

The Visuddhimagga is Theravada's encyclopedia of practice. Buddhaghosa synthesized generations of Mahāvihāra commentary into the definitive standard for the doctrine and practice of the entire Pali tradition.

It opens into three great parts along the three trainings—virtue (sīla), concentration (samādhi), wisdom (paññā). The concentration section catalogs forty meditation subjects (breath, foulness, the divine abodes, the kasiṇas) and the jhānas; the wisdom section contemplates the aggregates, elements, faculties, truths, and dependent origination, up to Nibbāna.

Running through it is the sequence of seven purifications—from purification of virtue and of mind onward through purity of view, of overcoming doubt, and so on—breaking the path into an operational, self-verifiable engineered ladder. This is Theravada's hallmark of graded clarity.

Cross-disciplinary Resonance

With cognitive training: the virtue-concentration-wisdom sequence maps to the modern "constraint—focus—insight" layering of skill acquisition. Set environmental limits to cut distraction (virtue), train stable attention (concentration), and only then can insight arise (wisdom). Skipping the first two to chase "sudden awakening" is mostly fantasy.

With attention neuroscience: the progression from "access concentration" to "absorption" tracks the attentional shift from wavering to lock-on; the forty subjects amount to a validated library of attention-training protocols.

Living Practice

Traditional: a practitioner selects one meditation subject per the Visuddhimagga, first purifies conduct, develops concentration to the corresponding jhāna, then turns to insight—all verified by a teacher.

Modern: transplant virtue-concentration-wisdom into your workflow—virtue: silence notifications, carve out a do-not-disturb block; concentration: 90 minutes of single-threaded deep work; wisdom: a calm retrospective seeing exactly how the mind got pulled away. One loop of three beats shouting "focus!" at yourself.

Daily Practice

One virtue-concentration-wisdom loop: pick one important task today. Do "virtue"—clear the desk, silence the phone; do "concentration"—25 uninterrupted minutes; then do "wisdom"—write three lines: "How often did I drift? What pulled me? How to set the boundary next time?" After a week, the mind's habits surface clearly.
Theravada · Abhidhamma Compendium

A Manual of Abhidhamma (Abhidhammattha-saṅgaha)

By Anuruddha · c. 11th–12th century CE

Scripture

"Tattha vuttābhidhammatthā, catudhā paramatthato; cittaṃ cetasikaṃ rūpaṃ, nibbānam iti sabbathā." Trans.: The matters of Abhidhamma taught here are, in the ultimate sense, fourfold: consciousness (citta), mental factors (cetasika), matter (rūpa), and Nibbāna—all is comprised in these. These four "ultimate realities" are Theravada's coordinate system for analyzing all experience.

Commentary

The seven books of the Abhidhamma Piṭaka are vast; this work is Anuruddha's distilled handbook, for a millennium the first textbook of Abhidhamma for Theravada monastics.

Its core distinction is between concept (paññatti) and ultimate reality (paramattha): table, self, and time are merely nominal concepts, further divisible; but consciousness, mental factors, matter, and Nibbāna are indivisible "ultimates"—a thoroughgoing reductive analysis. Consciousness is analyzed into 89 (or 121) types, with 52 associated mental factors (contact, feeling, perception, volition, greed, hatred, wisdom...).

Its subtlest teaching is the mind-moment (cittakkhaṇa): mind flows as an ultra-rapid succession of arising-persisting-vanishing instants, one cognitive process built of a fixed sequence of seventeen mind-moments. What we call "a single thought" is a torrent of momentary minds—impermanence dissected to its finest grain.

Cross-disciplinary Resonance

With consciousness studies: the succession of mind-moments echoes the discrete-frame hypothesis—cognitive science suggests perception may not be a continuous stream but updates in "perceptual moments" of tens of milliseconds. Two millennia ago, Abhidhamma had already deconstructed the "continuous self" into a sequence of instants.

With phenomenology: "ultimate vs. conceptual" approaches Husserl's phenomenological reduction—bracketing the natural attitude of a "substantial self," leaving only the mind-and-matter elements as they present now. The difference: Abhidhamma also deconstructs the "self that reduces."

Living Practice

Traditional: using Abhidhamma as a "map of mind," the meditator identifies in real time: "Which consciousness arises now? Which mental factors accompany it? Wholesome or unwholesome?"

Modern: when emotion surges, don't say "I'm upset"; parse it with the language of mental factors: "This is hatred (resistance), with restlessness (agitation) and a trace of conceit (feeling wronged)." Resolving a chaotic emotion into a few observable "dhammas" instantly loosens it from "me" into an observed object.

Daily Practice

Naming mental factors: when emotion stirs today, pause three seconds; without judging, simply "name"—tag the present mind with an Abhidhamma label (greed / hatred / conceit / envy / restlessness / faith / wisdom / compassion…). To name is to observe. Note three instances a day; before sleep, review: which factor knocks most often? That is your life's work.
Theravada · Living Tradition of Realization

Insight (Vipassanā) and Contemplation of Arising-Passing

Grounded in the Dhammapada and Visuddhimagga (Wisdom section) · Modern Burmese revival: Ledi, Mahasi, U Ba Khin–Goenka

Scripture

"Sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā ti, yadā paññāya passati; atha nibbindati dukkhe, esa maggo visuddhiyā." Trans.: When one sees with wisdom that "all formations are impermanent," one grows disenchanted with suffering—this is the path to purity. (Dhammapada 277; verses 278–279 add "all formations are suffering" and "all phenomena are non-self," together the three marks that insight observes.)

Commentary

Insight (vipassanā) is Theravada's core method: beyond the calming of serenity meditation (samatha), it uses a stable mind to observe reality as it is—seeing the three marks of impermanence, suffering, and non-self.

The Visuddhimagga's wisdom section lists sixteen insight knowledges, pivoting on the "knowledge of arising and passing (udayabbaya)"—directly witnessing each phenomenon arise and vanish moment by moment, and thence turning toward dispassion and liberation. This is not an inferred "impermanence" but one seen with one's own eyes.

Over the last century Burma sparked an insight revival: Ledi Sayadaw brought vipassanā to lay people; the Mahasi method takes the rise-and-fall of the abdomen as primary object and uses mental "noting"; the U Ba Khin–Goenka method sweeps bodily sensations (vedanā) and makes "non-reaction" to pleasant/unpleasant feeling the key. Different methods, one goal: direct seeing of the three marks.

Cross-disciplinary Resonance

With interoception: Goenka's "scanning bodily sensations" directly trains awareness of internal signals, localized in the brain to the insula. Studies show long-term insight practitioners have more active insulae and stronger emotion regulation—observing sensations arise and pass reshapes the body map.

With predictive coding: "non-reaction to feeling" severs the automatic chain "feeling → craving/aversion → action," matching the neural idea of interrupting the habitual predict-react loop: starve the prediction and the old pattern fades.

Living Practice

Traditional: in retreat, meditators alternate sitting and walking ten hours a day, scanning or noting, never conversing with thoughts, only watching them arise and pass.

Modern: ten minutes of body-scan daily—move attention slowly head to foot; meeting itch, ache, tightness, or numbness, only observe its arising and fading, neither grasping nor pushing away. When you watch an "unbearable" itch rise and pass on its own, you loosen your grip once on the iron law that "urges must be satisfied"—a steadiness that transfers to every "I can't help it" at work and in parenting.

Daily Practice

Watching one itch: if an itch arises while sitting, don't scratch. Put full attention on it: intensifying or fading? Sharp-edged? Staying or moving? Most itches subside within a minute or two on their own. Watch it arise, watch it pass—in that moment you verify "impermanence" and release one automatic reaction.
Theravada · Sutta · Majjhima Nikāya

Discourse on the Foundations of Mindfulness (Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta)

Spoken by the Buddha · Majjhima Nikāya 10 · parallel to the Chinese Madhyama Āgama "Discourse on Mindfulness"

Scripture

"Ekāyano ayaṃ, bhikkhave, maggo sattānaṃ visuddhiyā … nibbānassa sacchikiriyāya, yadidaṃ cattāro satipaṭṭhānā." Trans.: Monks, this is the "direct (one-going) path" for the purification of beings, for overcoming sorrow and lamentation, for the disappearance of pain and grief, for attaining the true way, for realizing Nibbāna—namely, the four foundations of mindfulness. It then unfolds as: contemplating the body in the body, feelings in feelings, mind in mind, and phenomena (dhammas) in phenomena—ardent, clearly comprehending, mindful, having removed covetousness and grief for the world.

Commentary

The Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta is the foundational text of Theravada meditation, giving the Buddha's own source for the preceding three (commentary, Abhidhamma, insight). The opening term "ekāyana" has been read as "the only path," "the direct path," and "the path going to the one (Nibbāna)"—all pointing to its pivotal role in liberation.

The four foundations are four fields of contemplation: body (breath, postures, body parts, foulness, four elements), feeling (pleasant, unpleasant, neutral), mind (states such as with/without greed, scattered/collected), and dhammas (the five hindrances, five aggregates, six sense bases, seven awakening factors, four truths). From coarse to subtle, outer to inner.

Worth distinguishing: this discourse (MN 10) and the Mahāsatipaṭṭhāna Sutta of Day 12 (DN 22) are largely identical, except the latter expands the analysis of the four noble truths under dhamma-contemplation. One concise, one extended—two recensions of the same teaching, which Theravada takes as the operational blueprint for joined serenity-and-insight.

Cross-disciplinary Resonance

With affective neuroscience: "contemplation of feeling" isolates vedanā—the raw hedonic tone of pleasant/unpleasant/neutral—for observation, matching science's core affect, the valence dimension prior to any named emotion. Catching feeling before the story is the best cut-point for breaking the emotion chain.

With metacognition: "contemplation of mind" is awareness of "what state mind is in now," i.e., metacognitive monitoring—stepping back to watch the thought rather than being lost in its content. This is the ancient prototype of "decentering" in psychotherapy.

Living Practice

Traditional: the practitioner follows the four in sequence—settling in the body (breath), observing feeling when disturbed, then the mind's greed and scatter, and finally seeing the five hindrances arise and pass under dhamma-contemplation.

Modern: in the instant of conflict, when anger rises, run a miniature four-foundations pass: body—notice the breath quickening, shoulders tightening; feeling—recognize this as an "unpleasant feeling"; mind—see that "aversion has arisen"; dhammas—recall "this is the hindrance of aversion, impermanent, it will pass." Four steps done, you have often switched from reaction to observation, and words won't fly out to wound.

Daily Practice

The four-foundations mini-run: whenever emotion stirs today, silently walk four steps—"body: where is the body tight? feeling: pleasant, unpleasant, or neutral? mind: what mind is this (greed/aversion/scatter)? dhamma: which hindrance is it, and is it impermanent?" Then decide whether to speak. Turning "reaction" into "first, four steps of observation" carries the foundations off the cushion into life.

Deeper Reflection

Dialogues between texts, doctrinal differences, cross-disciplinary mappings, the edges of practice
1. If Abhidhamma calls consciousness, mental factors, and matter indivisible "ultimate realities," isn't that reintroducing intrinsic nature—clashing with Madhyamaka emptiness?
This is the famous sectarian-vs-Mahāyāna divergence; don't force them together. Theravada posits "ultimate realities" as the endpoint of reductive analysis—the finest object for insight—yet still holds them momentary, impermanent, and non-self, not permanent substances. Madhyamaka (Day 2) presses further: if "ultimates" are clung to as having intrinsic nature, that is still grasping, so even dhammas are empty. Read it as: Theravada empties the "person-self" utterly, while its emptying of dhammas rests at the "impermanent/non-self" level; Madhyamaka pushes emptiness to "dhammas lack intrinsic nature." A difference of depth and sequence, not of right and wrong—clinging to Theravada "real dhammas" or Madhyamaka "blank nothingness" both err.
2. Mahasi's "noting" and Goenka's "body-sweep" are modern methods. Do they match the Pali Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta? Where is the edge of innovation?
See them as different engineered implementations of one principle. The classic four foundations give the principle (observe body-feeling-mind-dhammas as they are), not a rule that "you must silently note" or "must sweep the whole body." Modern teachers made the principle protocol-ized and reproducible so lay people can begin—this is the vitality of transmission, not deviation. Two yardsticks judge the edge: does it still aim at direct seeing of the three marks? Does it still resolve to "non-reaction of craving/aversion"? So long as the method serves "seeing impermanence as it is, and dispassion," formal differences are skillful means. But making the technique itself the goal (clinging to noting, chasing a "cleared-through" sensation) inverts ends and means and becomes new craving.
3. Does Theravada's thoroughgoing "non-self" contradict the Chinese tradition's tathāgatagarbha (Day 22), which speaks of Buddha-nature and true permanence?
Verbally opposed, doctrinally reconcilable—but not to be glibly flattened. Theravada strictly guards "all phenomena are non-self," wary of any relapse into a "true self." The tathāgatagarbha stream posits a Buddha-nature of "permanence, bliss, self, purity" as a debate of skillful vs. definitive meaning, countering nihilism and encouraging beings to awaken. The key to reconciliation: the "self" of tathāgatagarbha is a non-self self (suchness beyond the tetralemma) once nihilism is negated, not the substantial ātman of the heterodox—the Laṅkāvatāra (Day 17) uses "tathāgatagarbha as store-consciousness" precisely to prevent misreading. The contradiction lies at the level of language; brought to "freedom from all conceptual proliferation," they are not two. Yet present each tradition's stance faithfully; no need to judge one by the other.
4. Which comes first, serenity (samatha) or insight (vipassanā)? Is "dry insight" without deep absorption viable?
There is genuine tension within Theravada; just see it as it is. The mainstream (Visuddhimagga) holds serenity and insight in tandem, using access concentration or the jhānas as a stable platform for insight. The dry-insight vehicle (sukkhavipassaka) holds that mere "momentary concentration" suffices to begin insight—the Mahasi method is close to this, hence its popularity among lay people. The dispute is really about "how much concentration is enough for insight," not about presence or absence—with no concentration the mind scatters and cannot observe; with excess, one clings to jhānic bliss and never turns to insight. The safe path: cultivate "enough" concentration, then enter insight; when too scattered, supplement serenity; when clinging to bliss, turn to insight. Concentration and insight are two wheels of one cart, each supplied as it runs short.
5. Is the "arising-passing" seen in insight the same as cognitive science's "perceptual moments / frames of consciousness"?
A moving resonance, but not to be hastily equated. Similarities: both deny a "continuous self-stream," both deconstruct experience into a succession of discrete units, and both work at millisecond scales. Two differences: first, insight's "arising-passing" is first-person direct perception, while the perceptual frame is a third-person neural/behavioral inference, not necessarily the very "passing" the meditator beholds. Second, insight aims at dispassion and liberation, while science aims at explaining mechanism—different purposes. They can illuminate each other (e.g., insula studies corroborating the effect of observing feeling), but directly "translating" meditative experience into neural events, or using science to "prove" the Dharma, oversteps—precisely the "quantum-and-dependent-origination misuse" trap that Day 31 examines.
