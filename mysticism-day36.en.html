Prajñāpāramitā · Non-Duality

Vimalakīrti Sūtra: "The Dharma-Gate of Non-Duality" (vs. Advaita Vedanta)

Trans. Kumārajīva · 406 CE · Chapter 9

Scripture Passage

Mañjuśrī said: "As I see it, to be free of words and speech, of showing and knowing, apart from all questions and answers—this is entering the gate of non-duality." He then asked Vimalakīrti: "Each of us has spoken. Now, good sir, what is the bodhisattva's entry into non-duality?" Then Vimalakīrti kept silent, saying nothing. Mañjuśrī praised him: "Excellent, excellent! To be without even letters and words—this is truly to enter the gate of non-duality."

Commentary

Thirty-one bodhisattvas first name paired opposites (birth/death, defiled/pure, self/mine) to be transcended; Mañjuśrī names wordlessness itself; Vimalakīrti answers with a single silence—"Vimalakīrti's silence, loud as thunder," the summit of the Mahāyāna's dismantling of subject-object and language. Non-duality is not fusing two into a "Great One"; it refuses even "the One": there is no ultimate entity in which to rest.

This forms the subtlest contrast with Advaita Vedanta. Śaṅkara (8th c.) realizes non-duality through "Ātman = Brahman" and "not this, not this" (neti neti)—yet his non-duality affirms a permanent, pure, conscious ultimate Self as the ground of all things. Vimalakīrti's silence posits no ground: non-duality here just is dependent origination and emptiness. Both say "non-dual"; they point in opposite directions.

Cross-Disciplinary Notes

Philosophy of mysticism: W.T. Stace describes "introvertive mystical experience" as "pure consciousness, without object or content"—Vimalakīrti's and Śaṅkara's states look phenomenologically alike. But Steven Katz's constructivism counters: there is no "uninterpreted pure experience"; the experience is already shaped by its doctrine—which neatly explains why "one silence" yields two verdicts, self versus no-self.

Living Practice

Traditional: Chan uses "Vimalakīrti's silence" as a huatou, testing whether a student can meet the place where words fail—without falling into a new grasping at "some 'non-duality' to attain."

Modern: Next time you are trapped in either/or (be strict or lenient, advance or retreat), don't rush to "split the difference." First rest in that silence—let both poles loosen and see what this concrete situation actually needs. Non-duality is not a mushy midpoint; it is stepping out of the framing of opposition altogether.

Daily Cultivation

Silence practice: Today, take one person or event you habitually judge at once, and look at it for thirty seconds attaching no label (good/bad, right/wrong). Observe: when the impulse to judge stops, what remains? That awareness, uncut by concepts, is the doorway to non-duality.
Prajñāpāramitā · Heart of Emptiness

The Heart Sūtra (vs. Christian Contemplation)

Trans. Xuanzang · 649 CE · Essence of the Prajñā corpus

Scripture Passage

"Śāriputra, form is not different from emptiness, emptiness not different from form; form itself is emptiness, emptiness itself is form; feeling, perception, formations, consciousness are likewise. … Therefore in emptiness there is no form, no feeling-perception-formation-consciousness, no eye-ear-nose-tongue-body-mind, no form-sound-smell-taste-touch-object … no wisdom and no attainment. Because there is nothing to attain, the bodhisattva, relying on prajñāpāramitā, has a mind free of hindrance."

Commentary

The Heart Sūtra peels away in a cascade of "no's": the five aggregates, the twelve sense-fields, the eighteen elements, dependent origination, the Four Truths, even "wisdom" and "attainment"—nothing left to grasp. This is prajñā's apophasis: it never tells you "what reality is"; it only keeps negating everything you clutch.

The method is strikingly close to Christian apophatic theology (via negativa). Pseudo-Dionysius says God is "beyond every affirmation and negation"; The Cloud of Unknowing teaches veiling all things under a "cloud of forgetting"; Meister Eckhart seeks release into the "Godhead" (Gottheit)—all approaching the ineffable through "not this, not this." They share the method; they differ in destination: contemplation still ends in union with an ineffable "God"; the Heart Sūtra ends in "nothing to attain"—emptying even the emptiness that empties, leaving no ultimate existent.

Cross-Disciplinary Notes

Neuroscience: In deep contemplation/meditation, the default mode network (DMN)—the seat of self-narrative—drops markedly in activity. "A mind free of hindrance" and the "cloud of forgetting" may share a neural basis: momentarily releasing the region that ceaselessly weaves the "I."

Predictive processing: Treating the brain as a "prediction machine," the layered negations of "no eye-ear-nose-tongue-body-mind" resemble actively releasing successive priors, so experience is not carved up by prior categories—a cognitive-science metaphor for apophasis.

Living Practice

Traditional: The Heart Sūtra is the most widely recited text in Chinese Buddhism—chanted morning and evening, held in mind amid events, settling body and mind by "seeing the five aggregates as empty."

Modern: When anxiety rises, do a "Heart-Sūtra subtraction": take the thought "I'm a failure" apart—"I" is empty (just a dependently-arisen stream of body-mind), "failure" is empty (a label for one event), "so" is empty (emotional amplification). Not suppressing the feeling, but seeing that it is built of concepts—and the hindrance loosens.

Daily Cultivation

Subtraction chant: Today, quietly recite "form is emptiness, emptiness is form" a few times; with each round, put down one thing you are gripping (a worry, an identity, a "must"). Feel "because there is nothing to attain, the mind is unhindered"—the less attained, the more free.
Chan · Linji School

The Record of Linji (True Person of No Rank · vs. Sufi Fanā)

Taught by Linji Yixuan · comp. Huiran · 9th c. Tang

Scripture Passage

Ascending the hall, Linji said: "On this lump of red flesh there is a true person of no rank, constantly going in and out through the gates of your face (the senses). Those who have not yet witnessed it, look, look!" A monk stepped forward and asked: "What is the true person of no rank?" Linji got down from his seat, seized him, and said: "Speak! Speak!" As the monk hesitated, Linji shoved him away: "The true person of no rank—what a dried shit-stick he is!" and returned to his quarters.

Commentary

The "true person of no rank" is the blade of Linji's Chan: it strips away every identity and rank (holy/ordinary, monk/layperson, subject/object), forcing you to see directly the living awareness of this moment that occupies no role. Yet the instant you grasp it as "a real Self," Linji curses it as "a dried shit-stick"—not even "true person of no rank" may be made solid. This is Chan's "Great Death": total surrender of self-clinging; only when utterly dead does one come alive.

This runs deep against Sufi fanā (annihilation/extinction of the self). Through fanā the Sufi extinguishes the small self—al-Ḥallāj cries "I am the Truth (Anā al-Ḥaqq)," Rumi melts into God through love. They share the breaking of self: both demand the busy little "I" die. They differ in the aftermath: fanā is followed by baqā' (subsistence in God)—the self is extinguished and returns to God; Linji's Great Death returns to no God and no Great Self, only the "rankless" living function of dependent arising—extinction with nowhere to land, hence alive everywhere.

Cross-Disciplinary Notes

Neuroscience of ego-dissolution: "Ego-dissolution" is now a measurable state—in deep meditation and psychedelic experience, the DMN disintegrates and the self/other boundary breaks down; Carhart-Harris's "entropic brain" hypothesis describes this temporary collapse of the "I." "Great Death" and "fanā" may touch the same psychological break-point, but Buddhism insists the aftermath acknowledges no metaphysical ground as destination, guarding against re-fixing the experience as a new "Self/God."

Living Practice

Traditional: With shouts, blows, and "snatching away the environment," Linji cuts off the student's deliberation in a flash, forcing an immediate "loss of position."

Modern: When some "identity" hijacks you into suffering ("I'm a senior expert, I can't err"; "I'm a good mother, I must be perfect"), try asking: "Strip off the title—who is still here?" That which breathes, knows, and acts without relying on any title—that is the "true person of no rank." Identity is a tool, not a cage.

Daily Cultivation

One question of un-ranking: Today, in a moment when you feel "I must hold up some role," pause and ask silently: "Remove this identity—who is present now?" Don't search for an answer; just feel that awareness which depends on no title. Practice wearing identity loosely, rather than welding yourself to it.
Tathāgatagarbha · Yogācāra

Laṅkāvatāra Sūtra: "The Tathāgatagarbha Chapter" (Common & Uncommon: the Master Frame)

Trans. Guṇabhadra · 443 CE · Four-fascicle version

Scripture Passage

Mahāmati asked the Buddha: "World-Honored One, the sūtras speak of a tathāgatagarbha, pure by nature… Why does the World-Honored One's teaching resemble the non-Buddhists' doctrine of a Self, in speaking of a tathāgatagarbha?" The Buddha replied: "The tathāgatagarbha I teach is not the same as the Self of the non-Buddhists. At times I teach it as emptiness, signlessness, wishlessness… In order to remove the fools' dread of the phrase 'no-self,' I teach the gate of the tathāgatagarbha as a realm free of discrimination, without anything. … To draw in the non-Buddhists who cling to a Self, I teach the tathāgatagarbha, so that they may leave behind the false view of self."

Commentary

This chapter is the Buddha's own drawing of the "common and uncommon" line. The tathāgatagarbha (the pure awakened nature innate in all) sounds much like the Indian non-Buddhists' "Self" (Ātman), and like the "true Great Self / divine ground" of various mysticisms. Here the Buddha answers plainly: the tathāgatagarbha is skillful means, not the positing of a real Self. Its true import is "emptiness, signlessness, wishlessness"—a gateway for beings who fear "no-self," ultimately still returning to selfless emptiness.

This sets the standard for all cross-tradition comparison: Buddhism can share with Vedanta, contemplation, and Sufism the path and experience of "dissolving the small self, facing the ineffable," yet on ultimate ontology it holds the uncommon—affirming no permanent, self-existent, conscious metaphysical ground (whether named Brahman, God, Allah, or true Self). What is alike is the raft that crosses the river; what differs is the far shore reached.

Cross-Disciplinary Notes

Debates in philosophy of religion: This chapter is the Buddhist crux of the perennialism controversy—Huxley and others hold that all mystical traditions realize one ultimate Reality; the tathāgatagarbha's doctrinal caveat is precisely an internal "anti-perennialist" voice: experiences may be commensurable, ontological claims are not. This resonates with the pluralist stance that "different paths need not converge on the same summit."

Living Practice

Traditional: The Laṅkāvatāra was Bodhidharma's mind-sealing text; Chan uses the "tathāgatagarbha vs. no-self" distinction to keep practitioners from mistaking buddha-nature for a graspable "true Self."

Modern: In cross-cultural dialogue or interdisciplinary study, practice "deep respect where common, clear discernment where not": sincerely receive the depth of Christian contemplation, Sufi love, and Vedantic absorption (common), while seeing unambiguously how each asserts something different about "what the ultimate is" (uncommon). This mind—open yet precise—is the very art by which a "super-individual" crosses boundaries without losing roots.

Daily Cultivation

Discerning common from uncommon: Today, when you meet some claim that "all things return to One" or "all religions share one source," make two columns: on the left, "the experiences/methods it genuinely shares with the Dharma"; on the right, "how its claim about ultimate reality differs from selfless emptiness." Practice appreciating the common without blurring the line.

Deeper Reflections

1. Vimalakīrti's silence and Śaṅkara's Brahman look so alike in contemplative phenomenology—why does one say no-self and the other true Self? Can "shared experience" prove "shared ontology"?
No. This is the crux of Stace (experiential universalism) vs. Katz (constructivism). The same "objectless stillness" is read as "emptiness" within a no-self framework and as "realizing Brahman" within an Ātman framework—the interpretive frame shapes the experience's meaning. So similar states at most show that human consciousness has shared deep states; they cannot equate the traditions' metaphysical claims. The Buddhist position: experience may be common, ontology is not.
2. The Heart Sūtra's apophasis and Christian negative theology both walk "not this, not this," but one negates to "nothing to attain," the other to "union with God." Is this a real difference, or just different verbal packaging?
A real difference. Negative theology's "negation" is a means; the end is still to approach a God who is ineffable yet truly exists. The Heart Sūtra's negation is total—it empties even "the emptiness that can negate," presupposing no ultimate existent. One leads to "the fullness of God," the other to "the openness of dependent arising"—showing that "method" alone cannot determine "destination."
3. "Great Death" and Sufi "fanā" both dissolve the small self, and neuroscience measures an "ego-dissolution" state. If the experience can be induced, what unique value remains in Buddhist practice?
What remains is "how one settles after dissolution." Ego-dissolution is merely an inducible psychological state; Buddhism's distinctiveness lies not in producing it but in its view and ethics: after dissolution, not re-fixing the experience as a new "Self" or "God," and orienting it toward compassion and the wisdom of dependent arising. A drug may grant a glimpse but offers no path for "how to stop grasping, how to sustain it in daily life." The experience is a door; view and conduct are the road.
4. The Laṅkāvatāra calls the tathāgatagarbha a device to "draw in non-Buddhists," not a real Self. So when later "original enlightenment" thought is criticized as "close to the divine Self," is this the Buddha's device misread, or does this gate itself slide easily toward a true-Self doctrine?
Both. The text already warns itself: the tathāgatagarbha is contextual skillful means whose real import is emptiness and no-self; yet phrasings like "pure by nature, abiding awareness" are, in transmission, easily reified into a "permanent mind-only" close to Brahman-Self. Japanese "Critical Buddhism" questions original-enlightenment thought on just these grounds. The sound reading returns to the Laṅkāvatāra's own line: value its power to draw in and give confidence, while guarding it from sliding into a divine Self with "selfless emptiness"—skillful means must always be calibrated by the true import.
